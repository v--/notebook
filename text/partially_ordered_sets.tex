\section{Partially ordered sets}\label{sec:partially_ordered_sets}

\paragraph{Partial orders}

\begin{definition}\label{def:partially_ordered_set}\mcite[\S 1.1.3.1]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets}
  We call the \hyperref[def:binary_relation]{binary relation} \( \leq \) on a \hyperref[def:set]{set} \( P \) is a \term[ru=частичный порядок (\cite[def. 3.2]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток})]{partial order} if it is \hyperref[def:binary_relation/reflexive]{reflexive}, \hyperref[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitive} and \hyperref[def:binary_relation/antisymmetric]{antisymmetric}. The pair \( (P, \leq) \) is then called a \term[ru=часточно упорядоченное множество (\cite[def. 3.3]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток})]{partially ordered set}.

  We also call \( {\leq} \) an \term{order} (and \( (P, \leq) \) --- an \term{ordered set}) if we want to emphasize generality, e.g. a partially or \hyperref[def:totally_ordered_set]{totally ordered set} or a \hyperref[def:lattice]{lattice} or a \hyperref[def:banach_algebra]{Banach algebras} or \ldots.

  If \( x \leq y \), we say that \( x \) is \term{less than or equal} to \( y \) and that \( y \) is \term{greater than or equal} to \( x \). This terminology suggests that the strict relation \( {<} \), to be defined, is more fundamental, but from an abstract perspective this is not so, as we shall see.

  We introduce several auxiliary notions:
  \begin{thmenum}[series=def:partially_ordered_set]
    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/geq}\mcite[\S 1.1.3.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets} We also define \( \geq \) as the \hyperref[def:binary_relation/converse]{converse relation} of \( \leq \), with the obvious adjustment in terminology.

    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/strict}\mcite[\S 1.1.3.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets} We define the relation \( {<} \) as the difference of \( {\leq} \) and the \hyperref[def:binary_relation/diagonal]{diagonal relation} \( \Delta \), and distinguish it from \( {\leq} \) by calling it \term[bg=строго (неравенство) (\cite[10]{Проданов1982ФункционаленАнализЧаст1}), ru=строгий (порядок) (\cite[74]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток}), en=strict (order) (\cite[\S 1.1.3.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets})]{strict}.

    The terminology for \( < \) is simpler --- if \( x < y \), we say that \( x \) is \term{less than} \( y \) and that \( y \) is \term{greater than} \( x \).

    We can use the strict relation as \hyperref[con:primitive_notion]{primitive} instead --- see \cref{thm:strict_order_as_primitive}.

    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/comparable}\mcite[\S 1.1.6.1]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets} We call the elements \( x \) and \( y \) are \term{comparable} if either \( x \leq y \) or \( y \leq x \) and \term{incomparable} otherwise.
  \end{thmenum}

  Partially ordered sets have the following metamathematical properties:
  \begin{thmenum}[resume=def:partially_ordered_set]
    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/theory}\mimprovised Partially ordered sets can be described by a \hyperref[def:fol_theory]{first-order theory} over \hyperref[def:fol_signature/notation]{infix} binary \hyperref[def:fol_signature/predicate]{predicate symbol}, \( {\synleq} \), axiomatized by the reflexivity axiom \eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/reflexive}, the transitivity axiom \eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/transitive} and the antisymmetry axiom \eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/antisymmetric}.

    This is simple enough as it is, however we often wish to extend it with the aforementioned relations, introduced as \hyperref[def:fol_definition/predicate]{first-order definitions}:\fnote{Technically, we must take the \hyperref[def:fol_quantifier_closure]{universal closure} of these definitions.}
    \begin{thmenum}
      \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/theory/geq} The symbol \( {\syngeq} \) can be introduced as to obey
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/geq}
        \synx \syngeq \syny \syniff \syny \synleq \synx.
      \end{equation}

      \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict} The symbol \( {\synless} \) can be introduced as to obey
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}
        \synx \synless \syny \syniff ((\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny)).
      \end{equation}

      \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/theory/greater} Similarly, based on \( {\syngeq} \) rather than \( {\synleq} \), the symbol \( {\syngreater} \) can be introduced as to obey
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/greater}
        \synx \syngreater \syny \syniff ((\synx \syngeq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny)).
      \end{equation}
    \end{thmenum}

    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/morphism}\mcite[def. 1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets} We say that the function \( \varphi: (P, \leq_P) \to (Q, \leq_Q) \) between (partially) ordered sets is an \term[ru=порядковый гомоморфизм (\cite[92]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток}), en=homomorphism (\cite[\S 1.1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets})]{order homomorphism} if
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/morphism}
      x \leq_P y \T{implies} \varphi(x) \leq_Q \varphi(y).
    \end{equation}

    For the simple first-order signature with only \( {\synleq} \), these are the (weak) \hyperref[def:fol_homomorphism]{first-order homomorphisms}. If we also include \( {\synless} \) in the signature, we obtain \term{strict order homomorphisms}.

    When not dealing with categorical notions, we will often prefer terminology that is more explicit, like strict and nonstrict order-preserving and order-reversing maps. We discuss these notions in \cref{def:order_function}.

    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/category}\mcite[\S I.3.2]{Johnstone1982StoneSpaces} For a fixed \hyperref[def:grothendieck_universe]{Grothendieck universe} \( \mscrU \), the first-order theory described above naturally gives rise to a \hyperref[def:fol_theory_model_functor/obj]{category of \( \mscrU \)-small models}. We denote this category by \( \ucat{Pos} \).

    It is isomorphic to the category of \( \mscrU \)-small \hyperref[def:skeletal_category]{skeletal} preorder categories --- see \fullref{thm:order_category_isomorphism}.

    Strict homomorphisms lead to another category.

    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/subobject}\mimprovised Every subset of a (partially) ordered set, endowed with the restricted relation, is itself a (partially) ordered set.

    We call it an \term{ordered subset} if we need to highlight that it is not a plain set, however we often ignore the distinction with the underlying plain set.

    \thmitem{def:partially_ordered_set/opposite}\mcite[\S 1.19]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory} We define the \term{dual ordered set} of \( (P, \leq)^{\oppos} \) as \( (P, \geq) \). For the relation symbols themselves, we define \( \geq^{\oppos} \) as \( \leq \) and vice versa.

    This induces an \hyperref[con:opposite_object]{opposite object functor} in categories of (partially) ordered sets. Unlike in groups or rings, here we prefer the term \enquote{dual} to \enquote{opposite} because of \fullref{thm:order_duality}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item A partial order is defined as a reflexive, transitive and antisymmetric relation in
  \cite[4]{Engelking1989GeneralTopology},
  \cite[2]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory},
  \cite[13]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets},
  \cite[1]{Grätzer2011LatticeTheory},
  \cite[1]{Birkhoff1967LatticeTheory},
  \cite[72]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток} and
  \cite[10]{Проданов1982ФункционаленАнализЧаст1}.

  \Textcite[62]{Enderton1977SetTheory} takes strict orders as primitive, while \textcite[13]{Kelley1975GeneralTopology} defines partial orders as simply \hyperref[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitive} relations.
\end{comments}

\begin{definition}\label{def:inequality}\mimprovised
  The predicate application formulas in the \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set/theory]{first-order theory of preordered sets} are either \( \tau \synleq \sigma \) or \( \tau \syngeq \sigma \) for some terms \( \tau \) and \( \sigma \). We call such formulas \term{inequalities}.

  In the extended theory with symbols \( \synless \) and \( \syngreater \), we distinguish between the \term{strict inequality} \( \tau \synless \sigma \) and its corresponding \term{nonstrict inequality} \( \tau \syngeq \sigma \).

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:inequality/solution} As in the case of \hyperref[def:fol_equation]{equations}, we call the set \hyperref[def:fol_definability]{defined} by an inequality its \term{solution set}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:partially_ordered_set_theories}
  Over the \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set/theory]{signature of ordered sets} with only \( {\synleq} \) and \( {\synless} \), the following theories are \hyperref[def:interderivability]{interderivable} with respect to \hyperref[def:fol_natural_deduction]{classical first-order natural deduction}:

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/nonstrict} The nonstrict theory generated by \hyperref[def:binary_relation/reflexive]{reflexivity}, \hyperref[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitivity} and \hyperref[def:binary_relation/antisymmetric]{antisymmetry} of \( {\synleq} \) and the compatibility axiom \eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}.

    \thmitem{thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict} The strict theory generated by \hyperref[def:binary_relation/irreflexive]{irreflexivity} and \hyperref[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitivity} of \( {\synless} \) and the compatibility axiom
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict/compatibility}
      \synx \synleq \syny \syniff ((\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny)).
    \end{equation}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  \SufficiencySubProof Assume the nonstrict theory from \cref{thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/nonstrict}.

  \SubProof*{Irreflexivity of \( \synless \)}
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \synx]^u }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synx \synless \synx \syniff ((\synx \synleq \synx) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \synx)) }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_right}]{ (\synx \synleq \synx) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \synx) }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/elim_right}]{ \synneg (\synx \syneq \synx) }

      \infer0[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/intro}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/elim}]{ \synbot }
      \infer[left label={\( u \)}]1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/intro}]{ \synneg (\synx \synless \synx) }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/intro}]{ \qforall \synx \synneg (\synx \synless \synx) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}

  \SubProof*{Transitivity of \( \synless \)} Let \( P \) be the tree
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^u }
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}]^{a_2} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_right}]{ (\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/elim_left}]{ \synx \synleq \syny }

      \hypo{ [\syny \synless \synz]^v }
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}]^{a_2} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_right}]{ (\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/elim_left}]{ \syny \synleq \synz }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/intro}]{ \synx \synleq \syny \synwedge \syny \synleq \synz }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}

  We will use it twice. First, we will derive \( \synx \synless \synz \):
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/transitive}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ \substack{ [\synx \synless \syny]^v \\ [\syny \synless \synz]^w } }
      \ellipsis {\( P \)} { (\synx \synleq \syny \synwedge \syny \synleq \synz) \synimplies (\synx \synleq \synz) }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/imp/elim}]{ \synx \synleq \synz }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}

  Second, we will derive \( \synneg (\synx \syneq \synz) \):
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/antisymmetric}]^{a_2} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ \substack{ [\synx \synless \syny]^v \\ [\syny \synless \synz]^w } }
      \ellipsis {\( P \)} { \synx \synleq \syny \synwedge \syny \synleq \synz }

      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \synz]^b }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:thm:fol_natural_deduction_equality/symm}]{ \synz \syneq \synx }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synleq \syny \synwedge \syny \synleq \synx }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/imp/elim}]{ \synx \syneq \syny }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^v }
      \ellipsis{} { \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/elim}]{ \synbot }
      \infer[left label={\( b \)}]1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/intro}]{ \synneg (\synx \syneq \synz) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}

  \SubProof*{Compatibility of \( \synless \) with \( \synleq \)} To show \eqref{eq:thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict/compatibility}, we must demonstrate that each side can derive the other.

  In one direction, we derive \( \synx \synleq \syny \) from \( (\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny) \):
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [(\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny)]^u }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^b }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_right}]{ (\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }

      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/elim_left}]{ \synx \synleq \syny }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/reflexive}]^{a_2} }.
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}]{ \synx \synleq \synx }
      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synleq \syny }

      \infer[left label={\( b, c \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ \synx \synleq \syny }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize

  In the other direction, we derive \( (\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny) \) from \( \synx \synleq \syny \):
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \infer0[\ref{inf:thm:propositional_admissible_rules/lem}]{ (\synx \syneq \syny) \synvee \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }

      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^b }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_left}]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synleq \syny]^u }
      \hypo{ [\synneg (\synx \syneq \syny)]^c }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/intro}]{ (\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_left}]{ \synx \synless \syny }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_right}]{ \synanon }

      \infer[left label={\( b, c \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ (\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize

  \NecessitySubProof Assume the strict theory from \cref{thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/nonstrict}.

  \SubProof*{Reflexivity of \( \synleq \)}
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict/compatibility}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synx \synleq \synx \syniff ((\synx \synless \synx) \synvee (\synx \syneq \synx)) }

      \infer0[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/intro}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_right}]{ (\synx \synless \synx) \synvee (\synx \syneq \synx) }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_left}]{ \synx \synleq \synx }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/intro}]{ \qforall \synx (\synx \synleq \synx) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}

  \SubProof*{Transitivity of \( \synleq \)} Transitivity follows from
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \hypo{ [(\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny)]^u }

      \hypo{ \substack{ [(\syny \synless \synz) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synz)]^v \\ [\synx \synless \syny]^b } }
      \ellipsis{\( A \)} { \synanon }

      \hypo{ \substack{ [(\syny \synless \synz) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synz)]^v \\ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c } }
      \ellipsis{\( B \)} { \synanon }

      \infer[left label={\( b, c \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ (\synx \synless \synz) \synvee (\synx \syneq \synz) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  where \( A \) is
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [(\syny \synless \synz) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synz)]^v }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/transitive}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^b }
      \hypo{ [\syny \synless \synz]^d }
      \infer2{ (\synx \synless \syny) \synwedge (\syny \synless \synz) }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/imp/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synz }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^b }
      \hypo{ [\syny \syneq \synz]^e }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synz }

      \infer[left label={\( d, e \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synz }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_left}]{ (\syny \synless \synz) \synvee (\synx \syneq \synz) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize
  and \( B \) is
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [(\syny \synless \synz) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synz)]^v }

      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c }
      \hypo{ [\syny \synless \synz]^f }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synz }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_left}]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c }
      \hypo{ [\syny \syneq \synz]^g }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \syneq \synz }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_right}]{ \synanon }

      \infer[left label={\( f, g \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ (\synx \synless \synz) \synvee (\synx \syneq \synz) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize

  \SubProof*{Antisymmetry of \( \synleq \)} Antisymmetry follows from
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}
      \hypo{ [(\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny)]^u }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/irreflexive}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synneg (\synx \synless \synx) }

      \hypo{ \substack{ [(\syny \synless \synx) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synx)]^v \\ [\synx \synless \syny]^b } }
      \ellipsis{\( A \)} { \synx \synless \synx }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/elim}]{ \synbot }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/bot/efq}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }

      \hypo{ \substack{ [(\syny \synless \synx) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synx)]^v \\ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c } }
      \ellipsis{\( B \)} { \synx \syneq \synx }

      \infer[left label={\( b, c \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  where \( A \) is
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [(\syny \synless \synx) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synx)]^v }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/transitive}]^{a_2} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^b }
      \hypo{ [\syny \synless \synx]^d }
      \infer2{ (\synx \synless \syny) \synwedge (\syny \synless \synx) }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/imp/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synx }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^b }
      \hypo{ [\syny \syneq \synx]^e }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synx }

      \infer[left label={\( d, e \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synx }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize
  and \( B \) is
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [(\syny \synless \synx) \synvee (\syny \syneq \synx)]^v }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/irreflexive}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synneg (\synx \synless \synx) }

      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c }
      \hypo{ [\syny \synless \synx]^f }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synx }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/elim}]{ \synbot }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/bot/efq}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }

      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c }
      \hypo{ [\syny \syneq \synx]^g }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }

      \infer[left label={\( f, g \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ \synx \syneq \synx }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize

  \SubProof*{Compatibility of \( \synleq \) with \( \synless \)} To show \eqref{eq:def:partially_ordered_set/theory/strict}, we must demonstrate that each side can derive the other.

  In one direction, we derive \( \synx \synless \syny \) from \( (\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) \):
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict/compatibility}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [(\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny)]^u }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/elim_left}]{ \synx \synleq \syny }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_right}]{ (\synx \synless \syny) \synvee (\synx \syneq \syny) }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^b }

      \hypo{ [\synanon]^u }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/elim_right}]{ \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }
      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^c }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/elim}]{ \synbot }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/bot/efq}]{ \synx \synless \syny }

      \infer[left label={\( b, c \)}]3[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/elim}]{ \synx \synless \syny }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize

  In the other direction, we derive \( (\synx \synleq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) \) from \( \synx \synless \syny \):
  \small
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{prooftree}[separation=1em]
      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict/compatibility}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synanon }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^u }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/or/intro_left}]{ \synanon }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/iff/elim_left}]{ \synx \synless \syny }

      \hypo{ [\eqref{eq:def:binary_relation/irreflexive}]^{a_1} }
      \infer1[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/forall/elim}*]{ \synneg (\synx \synless \synx) }

      \hypo{ [\synx \synless \syny]^u }
      \hypo{ [\synx \syneq \syny]^b }
      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:fol_natural_deduction/equality/elim}]{ \synx \synless \synx }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/elim}]{ \synbot }
      \infer[left label={\( b \)}]1[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/neg/intro}]{ \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }

      \infer2[\ref{inf:def:propositional_natural_deduction/and/intro}]{ (\synx \syneq \syny) \synwedge \synneg (\synx \syneq \syny) }
    \end{prooftree}
  \end{equation*}
  \normalsize
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{thm:strict_order_as_primitive}
  Given a set \( P \) with an \hyperref[def:binary_relation/irreflexive]{irreflexive} and \hyperref[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitive} relation \( {<} \), we can define the nonstrict relation \( {\leq} \) via \eqref{eq:thm:partially_ordered_set_theories/strict/compatibility}, and \( (P, \leq) \) will be a \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially ordered set} whose strict order coincides with \( {<} \).
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  All the axioms are verified in \cref{thm:partially_ordered_set_theories}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Duality}

\begin{concept}\label{con:duality}
  A quintessential notion in mathematics is that of \term[en=duality (\cite[159]{Kuyk1977ComplementarityInMathematics})]{duality}. \Textcite[5]{ConwayGuy1998BookOfNumbers} describe it as follows:
  \begin{displayquote}
    A concept exhibits \enquote{duality} when it has two aspects or has a \enquote{dual} purpose.
  \end{displayquote}

  The authors only mention duality while going through the etymology of English words related to the number \( 2 \). A discussion focused on abstract mathematics can be found in \cite[\S III.19]{GowersEtAl2008PrincetonCompanion}, which starts as follows:
  \begin{displayquote}
    Duality is an important general theme that has manifestations in almost every area of mathematics. Over and over again, it turns out that one can associate with a given mathematical object a related, \enquote{dual} object that helps one to understand the properties of the object one started with. Despite the importance of duality in mathematics, there is no single definition that covers all instances of the phenomenon.
  \end{displayquote}

  More formally, we may regard any \hyperref[def:morphism_invertibility/involution]{involution} as a form of duality. Various \hyperref[con:opposite_object]{opposite objects} are described by involutions that invert the operands of a binary operation or relation.

  A duality may fail to be an involution like in the case of a \hyperref[rem:double_dual]{double dual} vector spaces, where \( V \) can be embedded in \( V^{**} \) but the two are not in general isomorphic.

  In some cases such as \fullref{thm:order_duality}, \fullref{thm:lattice_duality} and \fullref{thm:boolean_algebra_duality}, the syntax of the object language allows encoding the duality.
\end{concept}

\begin{theorem}[Principle of duality for ordered sets]\label{thm:order_duality}\mcite[thm. 1.20]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory}
  A generalization of \hyperref[def:elementary_equivalence]{elementary equivalence} holds for a \hyperref[def:preordered_set]{preordered set} and its \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set/opposite]{opposite}.

  Consider the \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set/theory]{first-order theory of preordered sets}. Denote by \( \varphi^{\oppos} \) the transformation of the formula \( \varphi \) in which we swap all instances of \( \synleq \) and \( \syngeq \) via \fullref{alg:fol_formula_signature_translation}. In the extended theory with strict inequalities we must also swap \( \synless \) and \( \syngreater \).

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:order_duality/assignment} For every preordered set \( (P, \leq) \), every \hyperref[def:fol_variable_assignment]{variable assignment} \( v \) into \( P \) and every formula \( \varphi \), we have
    \begin{equation*}
      \Bracks{\varphi}_{(P, \leq)}^v = \Bracks{\varphi^{\oppos}}_{(P, \geq)}^v.
    \end{equation*}

    \thmitem{thm:order_duality/equisatisfiable} The set \( \Gamma \) of formulas is \hyperref[def:equisatisfiability]{equisatisfiable} with \( \Gamma^{\oppos} \).

    \thmitem{thm:order_duality/tautology} The formula \( \varphi \) is a \hyperref[def:fol_semantics/tautology]{tautology} if and only if \( \varphi^{\oppos} \) is.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{theorem}
\begin{comments}
  \item Another form of this principle is formalized in \fullref{thm:order_category_isomorphism}.

  \item Variations of this principle include \fullref{thm:lattice_duality} and \fullref{thm:boolean_algebra_duality}.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  The denotations of \( \leq \) and \( \geq \) are swapped along with the corresponding symbols.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:duality}
  We list various manifestations of \hyperref[con:duality]{duality}:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The multitude of \hyperref[con:opposite_object]{opposite objects} listed in \cref{rem:con:opposite_object}.

    \item \Fullref{alg:propositional_formula_dualization} and \fullref{alg:propositional_formula_dualization}.

    \item \hyperref[def:dual_vector_space]{Dual} and \hyperref[rem:double_dual]{double dual} \hyperref[def:vector_space]{vectors spaces}, as well as \hyperref[def:continuous_dual_space]{continuous dual spaces}.
  \end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Preordered sets}

\begin{definition}\label{def:preordered_set}\mcite[def. 1.3.1]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets}
  We call the \hyperref[def:binary_relation]{binary relation} \( \leq \) on a \hyperref[def:set]{set} \( P \) is a \term[bg=преднаредба (\cite[9]{Проданов1982ФункционаленАнализЧаст1}), ru=предпорядок (\cite[def. 3.1]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток})]{preorder} if it is \hyperref[def:binary_relation/reflexive]{reflexive} and \hyperref[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitive}. The pair \( (P, \leq) \) is the called a \term[en=preordered set (\cite[119]{Golan1999Semirings})]{preordered set}.
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item The precise relation with partially ordered sets is given in \cref{thm:preordered_set_antisymmetry}.

  \item Do do not study preordered sets systematically because many of the concepts commonly introduced for partially ordered sets are ill-behaved. See \cref{ex:def:preordered_set}.

  \item The term \enquote{preorder} (\enquote{\textru{предпорядок}} in Russian) is used in
  \cite[133]{Bourbaki2004TheoryOfSets},
  \cite[3]{Grätzer2011LatticeTheory},
  \cite[\S 5.4.2]{Mimram2020ProgramEqualsProof},
  \cite[10]{ЦаленкоШульгейфер1974ОсновыТеорииКатегорий}
  \cite[def. 3.1]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток}  and
  \cite[66]{БелоусовТкачёв2004ДискретнаяМатематика}.

  Another possibility is \enquote{quasi-order}, used in
  \cite[20]{Birkhoff1967LatticeTheory} and
  \cite[445]{Rotman2015AdvancedModernAlgebraPart1}.

  \item This concept is unrelated to pre-order enumeration of \hyperref[def:ordered_tree]{ordered trees} defined in \cref{def:ordered_tree_enumeration}.
\end{comments}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:preordered_set_antisymmetry}
  A \hyperref[def:preordered_set]{preordered set} \( (P, \leq) \) is \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially ordered} if and only if there exist \emph{distinct} elements \( x \) and \( y \) of \( P \) such that \( x \leq y \) and \( y \leq x \).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  This is a restatement of the negation of antisymmetry.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}\label{ex:def:preordered_set}
  We list some examples of \hyperref[def:preordered_set]{preordered sets}:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:preordered_set/integers} As shown in \cref{thm:semiring_divisibility_order}, the \hyperref[def:semiring_divisibility]{divisibility relation} in a \hyperref[def:semiring]{semiring} is a \hyperref[def:preordered_set]{preorder}.

    This is trivial to verify on \hyperref[def:integers]{integers}. Every number divides itself, so reflexivity holds, and a multiple of a multiple of \( n \) is itself a multiple of \( n \), so transitivity holds.

    The integers also allow us to verify \cref{thm:preordered_set_antisymmetry} because \( n \mid -n \mid n \), yet \( n \neq -n \).

    This largely stultifies the concept of strict orders because, if \( {<} \) was a strict order based on \( {\mid} \), then transitivity would imply \( n < n \).

    \thmitem{def:preordered_set/formulas} A \hyperref[def:consequence_relation]{consequence relation} \( {\vdash} \) on a set \( S \) is a preorder because of \eqref{eq:def:consequence_relation/reflexivity} and \eqref{eq:def:consequence_relation/transitivity}.

    We will again demonstrate the lack of antisymmetry. For example, \cref{thm:syntactic_minimal_tautologies} demonstrates how, for any \hyperref[def:propositional_formula]{propositional formula} \( \varphi \), the law of non-contradiction
    \begin{equation*}
      \synneg (\varphi \synwedge \synneg \varphi)
    \end{equation*}
    can be derived without assumptions in the \hyperref[def:propositional_natural_deduction]{minimal natural deduction system}. \Cref{thm:propositional_natural_deduction_tautologies} shows that it must be \hyperref[def:interderivability]{interderivable} with \( \syntop \).

    Therefore, we have
    \begin{equation*}
      \syntop \vdash \synneg (\varphi \synwedge \synneg \varphi) \vdash \syntop.
    \end{equation*}

    A strict entailment relation would make no sense, and other concepts like suprema would lack uniqueness.

    For logical formulas, these issues are mitigated by taking the \hyperref[def:antisymmetric_quotient]{antisymmetric quotient} of \( (\op*{Form}, \vdash) \). The obtained partially ordered set is called a \hyperref[def:lindenbaum_tarski_algebra]{Lindenbaum-Tarski algebra} and is studied in \fullref{sec:propositional_completeness}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{example}

\begin{definition}\label{def:antisymmetric_quotient}\mimprovised
  Let \( (P, \leq) \) be a \hyperref[def:preordered_set]{preordered set}. Define the relation
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:antisymmetric_quotient}
    x \sim y \T{if and only if} x \leq y \T{and} y \leq x.
  \end{equation}

  On the \hyperref[def:equivalence_relation/quotient]{quotient set} \( Q \coloneqq P / {\sim} \), define the relation \( [x] \preceq [y] \) to hold if \( x \leq y \). The pair \( (Q, \preceq) \) is then a \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially ordered set}, which we will call the \term{antisymmetric quotient} of \( (P, \leq) \).
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item The relation \( \sim \) is the intersection of the relation \( \leq \) with its \hyperref[def:binary_relation/converse]{inverse}.

  \item We may prefer, as in the case of \cref{thm:lindenbaum_tarski_theories}, to refer to the projection operator \( \pi: P \to Q \) rather than to quotient sets.

  \item In the language of graph theory, this is expressed as \hyperref[def:directed_graph_condensation]{graph condensation}, where we contract each \hyperref[def:graph_cycle]{cycle} into a single vertex, at which point the graph becomes acyclic.
\end{comments}
\begin{defproof}
  First, we must show that the relation \( \preceq \) is well-defined. Indeed, let \( x \sim x' \) and \( y \sim y' \). If \( x \leq y \), we have \( x' \leq x \leq y \leq y' \), hence \( x' \leq y' \) because of transitivity.

  It is then clear that \( \preceq \) is a partial order because it inherits reflexivity and transitivity from \( \leq \) and antisymmetry is imposed by taking quotient sets --- equality in \( Q \) holds precisely when \( {\sim} \) holds in \( P \).

  Thus, \( (Q, \preceq) \) is indeed a partially ordered set.
\end{defproof}

\paragraph{Functions between ordered sets}

\begin{definition}\label{def:order_function}\mimprovised
  Let \( (P, \leq_P) \) and \( (Q, \leq_Q) \) be \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially order sets}. We will discuss some notions related to \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{order homomorphisms}.

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:order_function/preserving}\mimprovised We call the function \( f: P \to Q \) \term[en=order-preserving (map) (\cite[def. 1.34]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory})]{order-preserving} if
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:order_function/preserving}
      x \leq_P y \T{implies} f(x) \leq_Q f(y).
    \end{equation}

    If the inequalities are strict, i.e. if
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:order_function/preserving/strict}
      x <_P y \T{implies} f(x) <_Q f(y),
    \end{equation}
    we call \( f \) \term{strictly order-preserving}.

    Nonstrict order-preserving maps do not rely on antisymmetry and thus can be generalized to preordered sets.

    It will sometimes be convenient to order-preserving maps \term[ru=возрастающая (функция) (\cite[\S 47]{Фихтенгольц1968ОсновыАнализаТом1}), en=ascending (mapping) (\cite[def. 1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets})]{ascending} (resp. \term{nondescending}) or \term[en=increasing (mapping) (\cite[def. 1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets})]{increasing} (resp. \term[ru=неубывающая (функция) (\cite[\S 47]{Фихтенгольц1968ОсновыАнализаТом1})]{nondecreasing}).

    \thmitem{def:order_function/reversing} Dually, we call \( f: P \to Q \) \term[en=order-reversing map (\cite[\S 3.17]{Schechter1997AnalysisHandbook})]{order-reversing} if
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:order_function/reversing}
      x \leq_P y \T{implies} f(x) \geq_Q f(y).
    \end{equation}
    and or \term{strictly order-reversing} if
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:order_function/reversing/strict}
      x <_P y \T{implies} f(x) >_Q f(y).
    \end{equation}

    It will sometimes be convenient to order-reversing maps \term[ru=убывающая (функция) (\cite[\S 47]{Фихтенгольц1968ОсновыАнализаТом1}), en=descending (mapping) (\cite[def. 1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets})]{descending} (resp. \term{nonascending}) or \term[en=decreasing (mapping) (\cite[def. 1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets})]{decreasing} (resp. \term[ru=невозрастающая (функция) (\cite[\S 47]{Фихтенгольц1968ОсновыАнализаТом1})]{nonincreasing}).

    \thmitem{def:order_function/monotone}\mcite[def. 1]{Bourbaki2004TheoryOfSets} In some cases like \fullref{ch:real_analysis} or \fullref{ch:measure_theory}, we will collectively refer to either order-preserving or order-reversing maps as \term{monotone}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item Nonstrict order-preserving maps are used extensively in the theory of \hyperref[sec:partially_ordered_sets]{partially ordered sets}, in particular in \hyperref[sec:lattices]{lattice theory}, while strict order-preserving maps are used in the theory of \hyperref[sec:partially_ordered_sets]{totally ordered sets}, in particular for \hyperref[sec:ordinals]{ordinals}.

  \item An alternative term for order-preserving maps is \enquote{isotone}; it is preferred in
  \cite[2]{Birkhoff1967LatticeTheory},
  \cite[30]{Grätzer2011LatticeTheory},
  \cite[def. 1.9.2]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets},
  \cite[def. 3.9]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток} (as \enquote{\textru{изотонное изображение}}).

  For order-reversing maps, the equivalent is \enquote{antitone}; it is used in \cite[30]{Grätzer2011LatticeTheory}. In \cite[2]{Birkhoff1967LatticeTheory} \enquote{antitone} is instead used for order-reversing isomorphisms.
\end{comments}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:monotone_function_terminology}
  In elementary real analysis, a \enquote{monotone function} is either an increasing or decreasing real-valued function. For example, this is defined explicitly in
  \cite[12]{Folland1999RealAnalysis},
  \cite[def. 9.8.1]{Tao2022AnalysisI},
  \cite[\S 47]{Фихтенгольц1968ОсновыАнализаТом1},
  \cite[192]{Натансон1974ВещественныйАнализ},
  \cite[126]{Зорич2019АнализЧасть1} and
  \cite[199]{Тагамлицки1971ДиференциалноСмятане}.

  In more abstract real analysis, monotonically increasing functions are generalized to \hyperref[def:banach_space]{Banach spaces} and called simply \enquote{monotone operators}; see \cref{def:banach_space_monotone_operator}.

  Other authors call arbitrary \hyperref[def:order_function/preserving]{order-preserving} (but not order-reversing) functions \enquote{monotone}. This includes
  \cite[30]{Grätzer2011LatticeTheory},
  \cite[def. 1.34(i)]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory},
  \cite[6]{Aigner1997CombinatorialTheory}
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:def:order_function}
  The functions between ordered sets discussed in \cref{def:order_function} have the following basic properties:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:def:order_function/injective_implies_strict} Every \hyperref[def:function_invertibility/injective]{injective} order-preserving map is strict.

    The converse holds for totally ordered sets --- see \cref{thm:def:totally_ordered_set/embedding_iff_strict}.

    \thmitem{thm:def:partially_ordered_set/morphism_is_reflecting} An injective function between (pre)ordered sets is a \hyperref[def:fol_embedding]{first-order embedding} if and only if it is both \hyperref[def:order_function/preserving]{order-preserving} and \hyperref[def:order_function/preserving]{order-reflecting}.

    \thmitem{thm:def:order_function/comparables_reflect_inequalities} For any order-preserving map \( f: P \to Q \), if \( x \) and \( y \) are comparable elements, then \( f(x) < f(y) \) implies \( x < y \).
  \end{thmenum}
\end{proposition}
\begin{defproof}
  \SubProofOf{thm:def:order_function/injective_implies_strict} Let \( f: P \to Q \) be an injective order-preserving map.

  Let \( x <_P y \) for some \( x \) and \( y \) from \( P \). Since \( f \) is order-preserving, we have \( f(x) \leq_Q f(y) \). Since it is also injective, \( f(x) = f(y) \) implies \( x = y \), hence it remains for \( f(x) <_Q f(y) \) to hold.

  Generalizing on \( x \) and \( y \), we conclude that \( f \) is strict.

  \SubProofOf{thm:def:partially_ordered_set/morphism_is_reflecting} Let \( f: P \to Q \) be an injective function between preordered sets.

  \SufficiencySubProof* Suppose that \( f \) is an embedding, that is, an injective order-preserving map whose inverse is order-preserving.

  If \( f(x) \leq_Q f(y) \), then
  \begin{equation*}
    x = f^{-1}(f(x)) \leq_P f^{-1}(f(y)) = y.
  \end{equation*}

  We conclude that \( f \) is order-reflecting.

  \NecessitySubProof* Suppose that \( f \) is both order-preserving and order-reflecting.

  If \( f(x) \leq_Q f(y) \), then
  \begin{equation*}
    f^{-1}(f(x)) = x \leq_P y = f^{-1}(f(y)).
  \end{equation*}

  We conclude that \( f^{-1} \) is order-preserving.

  \SubProofOf{thm:def:order_function/comparables_reflect_inequalities} Suppose that \( x \) and \( y \) are comparable and let \( f(x) <_Q f(y) \).

  If \( x \geq y \), then \( y \leq x \) and thus \( f(y) \leq f(x) < f(y) \), which contradicts irreflexivity. It remains for \( x < y \) to hold.
\end{defproof}

\paragraph{Atoms}

\begin{definition}\label{def:partial_order_covering}\mcite[\S 1.14]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory}
  In a \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially ordered set} \( (P, \leq) \), we say that the element \( y \) \term{covers} \( x \) if \( x < y \) and there is no element \( z \) such that \( x < z < y \).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}\label{def:partial_order_atom}\mimprovised
  In a \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially ordered set} \( (P, \leq) \) with a \hyperref[def:extremal_points/top_and_bottom]{bottom element} \( \bot \), call \( x \) an \term[ru=атом (\cite[89]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток}), en=atom (\cite[113]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory})]{atom} if \( x \) \hyperref[def:partial_order_covering]{covers} \( \bot \).

  Dually, if \( P \) has a top element \( \top \), we call \( x \) a \term{coatom} if \( \top \) covers \( x \).
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item The definition is based on \cite[113]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory}, where atoms are defined in lattices. We generalize this and additionally define coatoms.
\end{comments}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:atoms_are_incomparable}
  Distinct \hyperref[def:partial_order_atom]{atoms} are incomparable.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  If \( x \) and \( y \) are distinct atoms, they are not comparable --- \( x < y \) implies that \( \bot < x < y \), which contradicts the assumption that \( y \) covers \( \bot \).
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Cartesian products of ordered sets}

\begin{definition}\label{def:product_order}\mcite[def. 4.1.6]{Harzheim2005OrderedSets}
  The \hyperref[def:fol_direct_product]{first-order direct product} of the family \( \seq{ (P_k, \leq_k) }_{k \in \mscrK} \) of \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{partially ordered sets} is itself a partially ordered set whose order is
  \begin{equation*}
    \seq{ x_k }_{k \in \mscrK} \leq \seq{ y_k }_{k \in \mscrK} \T{if and only if} \qforall* {k \in \mscrK} {(x_k \leq_k x_k)}.
  \end{equation*}

  We call \( {\leq} \) the \term{product order} in \( \prod_{k \in \mscrK} P_k \).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}\label{def:lexicographic_order}\mimprovised
  Let \( (P, \leq_P) \) and \( (Q, \leq_Q) \) be partially ordered sets.

  We define the \term[en=lexicographic order (\cite[18]{DaveyPriestley2002LatticeTheory}), ru=лексикографический (порядок) (\cite[99]{Гуров2013ТеорияРешёток})]{lexicographic order} on \( P \times Q \) as
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:lexicographic_order}
    (a, b) \prec (c, d) \thickspace \T{if and only if} \thickspace (a <_P c \T{or} (a = c \T{and} b <_Q d))
  \end{equation}
  and the \term{reverse lexicographic order} as
  \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:lexicographic_order/reverse}
    (a, b) \prec (c, d) \thickspace \T{if and only if} \thickspace (b <_Q d \T{or} (b = d \T{and} a <_P c)).
  \end{equation}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item We can use natural number recursion to extend this to arbitrary \( n \)-tuples.
\end{comments}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:lexicographic_order_is_partial_order}
  The \hyperref[eq:def:lexicographic_order]{lexicographic} and \hyperref[eq:def:lexicographic_order/reverse]{reverse lexicographic} orders are \hyperref[def:partially_ordered_set]{strict partial order} relations.
\end{proposition}
\begin{comments}
  \item An analogous result holds for total orders --- see \cref{thm:def:totally_ordered_set/lexicographic} --- and well-ordered sets --- see \cref{thm:def:well_ordered_set/lexicographic}.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  \SubProofOf[def:binary_relation/irreflexive]{irreflexivity} Trivial.

  \SubProofOf[def:binary_relation/transitive]{transitivity} Let \( \prec \) be a lexicographic order on \( P \times Q \).

  If \( (a, b) \prec (c, d) \) and \( (c, d) \prec (e, f) \), then
  \begin{itemize}
    \item If \( a < c \), then \( a < c \leq e \) and thus \( (a, b) \prec (e, f) \).

    \item If \( a = c \) and \( b < d \), then \( a \leq e \) and \( b < d \leq f \) and thus \( (a, b) \prec (e, f) \).
  \end{itemize}

   The proof for the reverse lexicographic order is analogous.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}\label{ex:def:lexicographic_order}
  We list several examples of \hyperref[def:lexicographic_order]{lexicographic ordering}:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/words} In a real-world dictionary like a thesaurus, the word \enquote{bright} comes after \enquote{blight}.

    Denote the English alphabet by \( A \). Then the lexicographic ordering on the Cartesian power \( A^6 \) suggests that since the first letters of \enquote{bright} and \enquote{blight} are equal, we should compare the second letters, which would lead us to the usual thesaurus order.

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/heterogenous_words} Real-world dictionaries list words with different numbers of letters. It is not clear how to compare \enquote{car} to \enquote{carboy}.

    We can add a sentinel symbol like \enquote{\( \cdot \)} to the English alphabet \( A \) to denote empty spaces. Then \enquote{car} in \( A^6 \) becomes \enquote{car\( \cdot \cdot \cdot \)}. Other situations like \cref{ex:def:lexicographic_order/natural_numbers} may require prepending \( \cdot \) rather than appending it.

    Now, if we specify how \( \cdot \) should compare to the letters, we can use lexicographic ordering to compare the two words. For example, making \( \cdot \) the bottom of the alphabet will make \enquote{car} precede \enquote{carboy}, while making it the top would do the converse.

    Note that this is only a problem if one word is a prefix of another. We can easily conclude that \enquote{apple} is smaller than \enquote{apricot} because the third letter \( p \) is smaller than \( r \).

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/natural_numbers} Consider the language of natural numbers in decimal notation from \cref{def:positional_number_system/decimal}. Ordering single-digit strings is trivial, but ordering multi-digit numeric strings requires using lexicographic ordering.

    Furthermore, even though it is clear that \( \syn1 \syn0 < \syn1 \syn1 \), handling numeric strings of differing lengths requires prepending a sentinel symbol \( \synmult \) that is less than any numeral. Then we have \( \synmult \syn9 < \syn1 \syn0 \), which corresponds to the natural number ordering discussed in \cref{def:peano_arithmetic_ordering}.

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/rectangle} A slightly more relevant example for mathematics is the lexicographic ordering
    \begin{equation*}
      AB < AD < BC < CD
    \end{equation*}
    of the names of the edges of a \hyperref[def:parallelogram/rectangle]{rectangle}. The reverse lexicographic ordering is
    \begin{equation*}
      AB < BC < AD < CD.
    \end{equation*}

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/triangle} For the sides of a \hyperref[def:triangle]{triangle}, we have \( AB < AC < BC \) for both orderings.

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/antichain} The antichain \eqref{eq:ex:def:partial_order_connectedness/quad_power_set/antichain} is ordered with respect to the lexicographic ordering on the Cartesian square of \( \set{ a, b, c, d } \).

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/graph} The edges of the graph in \eqref{eq:fig:def:directed_multigraph} are numbered in lexicographic order, which also happens to be the reverse lexicographic order.

    \thmitem{ex:def:lexicographic_order/ordinals} For \hyperref[def:ordinal_arithmetic]{ordinal arithmetic}, \Cref{thm:ordinal_addition_disjoin_union} and \cref{thm:ordinal_multiplication_cartesian_product} contain more interesting applications of lexicographic orders.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{example}
