\section{Algebras over rings}\label{sec:algebras_over_rings}

\paragraph{Algebras over rings}

\begin{definition}\label{def:algebra_over_ring}\mimprovised
  Fix a \hyperref[def:ring/commutative]{commutative ring} \( R \). An \( R \)-\term[bg=линейна асоциативна алгебра (над поле) (\cite[298]{ГеновМиховскиМоллов1991Алгебра}), ru=алгебра с единицей (над полем) (\cite[def. 1.7.1]{Винберг2014КурсАлгебры}), en=algebra (\cite[def. 3.1.1]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3})]{algebra} is an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:module]{module} \( M \) with an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:multilinear_function]{bilinear} vector multiplication operation. As with \hyperref[def:algebra_over_semiring]{algebras over semirings}, we denote the multiplication operation via either juxtaposition, \( {\cdot} \) or \( {\ast} \) and assume by default that a multiplicative unit exists.

  The main difference compared to algebras over semirings is that factorization is better behaved. We list the basic metamathematical properties:
  \begin{thmenum}[resume=def:algebra_over_ring]
    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/theory}\mimprovised Just like we extend the \hyperref[def:semimodule/theory]{theory of left semimodules} in \cref{def:algebra_over_semiring/theory}, here we must extend the \hyperref[def:semimodule/theory]{theory of left modules}, where we include subtraction but exclude absorption.

    As with algebras over semimodules, we need the function symbol \( {\synmult} \) and the unaltered distributivity axioms \eqref{eq:def:semiring/theory/axioms/distributivity/left} and \eqref{eq:def:semiring/theory/axioms/distributivity/right} from the theory of \hyperref[def:semiring/theory]{(semi)rings}, as well as the homogeneity axioms \eqref{eq:def:algebra_over_semiring/theory/homogeneity/left} and \eqref{eq:def:algebra_over_semiring/theory/homogeneity/left}.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/morphism}\mcite[def. 3.1.2]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} We say that the function \( \varphi: M \to N \) between \( R \)-algebras is \( R \)-\term[en=homomorphism (\cite[def. 3.1.2]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3})]{algebra homomorphism} if it is an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:module/morphism]{module homomorphism} that also preserves vector multiplication.

    If both \( M \) and \( N \) have multiplicative units and if \( \varphi \) preserves them, we call \( \varphi \) \term{unital}.

    The conditions stated are equivalent to those given in \cref{def:semimodule/morphism} for \( R \)-semimodule homomorphisms, however the reasoning that these homomorphisms coincide with the corresponding \hyperref[def:fol_homomorphism]{first-order homomorphism} is different. The difference amounts to absorption and additive inverses, both of which are discussed in \cref{def:module/morphism} for \( R \)-module homomorphisms.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/category}\mimprovised As with algebras over semirings, for a fixed \hyperref[def:grothendieck_universe]{Grothendieck universe} \( \mscrU \) and a fixed ring \( R \), the first-order theories of \( R \)-algebras gives rise to several \hyperref[def:fol_theory_model_functor/obj]{categories of \( \mscrU \)-small models}.

    Because of the different possibilities, we give no special notation like \( \ucat[R]{Alg} \) to these categories.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/submodel}\mcite[429]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} An \( R \)-\term[bg=подалгебра (\cite[305]{ГеновМиховскиМоллов1991Алгебра}), ru=подалгебра (\cite[66]{Кострикин2020АлгебраЧасть2})]{subalgebra} of \( M \) is an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:module/subobject]{submodule} that is also closed under vector multiplication. It makes sense to also consider \term{unital subalgebras} that contain the multiplicative unit.

    The same remarks apply as with \hyperref[def:algebra_over_semiring]{algebras over semirings}.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/generated}\mcite[429]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} We define the \term{generated \( R \)-subalgebra} of the a subset \( A \) of \( M \) as the intersection of \( R \)-subalgebras of \( M \) containing \( A \).

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/trivial} The \hyperref[def:module/trivial]{zero \( R \)-module} is vacuously an algebra, and we call it the \term{zero \( R \)-algebra}.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/ideal}\mcite[430]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} As in algebras over semirings, we call the \( R \)-subalgebra \( I \) of \( M \) a (left, right or two-sided) \( R \)-\term[en=ideal (\cite[430]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3})]{algebra ideal} if it is closed under (left, right or two-sided) vector multiplication with members of \( M \).

    The same remarks apply --- a \hyperref[def:ring/ideal]{ring ideal} \( I \) of a unital associative \( R \)-algebra \( M \) is automatically an \( R \)-algebra ideal.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/monoids}\mimprovised Similarly to rings, unital associative \( R \)-algebras have an additive and multiplicative monoid, and corresponding groups.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/associative}\mcite[def. 3.1.1]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} As in the case of algebras over semirings, we call an \( R \)-algebra \term{associative} if its multiplication is associative.

    An associative unital \( R \)-algebra is itself a ring and can be regarded as an extension of \( R \).

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/commutative}\mcite[def. 3.1.1]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} As in the case of algebras over semirings, we call an \( R \)-algebra over a ring \( R \) \term{commutative} if its multiplication is commutative.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/kernel} We define the \term{kernel} of an \( R \)-algebra homomorphism \( \varphi: M \to N \) as the set
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:def:algebra_over_ring/kernel}
      \ker \varphi \coloneqq \varphi^{-1}(0_N) = \set{ x \in M \given \varphi(x) = 0_N }.
    \end{equation}

    The kernel is a two-sided \( R \)-algebra ideal of \( M \) as a consequence of \cref{thm:kernel_is_algebra_ideal}.

    \thmitem{def:algebra_over_ring/quotient}\mimprovised \hyperref[def:fol_congruence]{First-order congruences} in the theory of nonunital \( R \)-algebras correspond to two-sided algebra \( R \)-algebra ideals.

    For this reason, a \term[en=quotient algebra (\cite[430]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3})]{quotient algebra} \( R / I \) is more conveniently defined with respect to a two-sided ideal \( I \) rather than with respect to the corresponding congruence.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item We discuss some discrepancies in this definition in \cref{rem:ring_unitality}.
\end{comments}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:ring_unitality}
  Whether \hyperref[def:semiring]{(semi)rings} and \hyperref[def:algebra_over_semiring]{algebras over them} are by default unital has important consequences. We discuss a few common conventions:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{rem:ring_unitality/unital} Rings are assumed to be unital by
    \cite[def. 1.8.1]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3},
    \cite[def. 2.1]{Jacobson1985BasicAlgebraI},
    \cite[83]{Lang2002Algebra},
    \cite[11]{Eisenbud1995CommutativeAlgebra} and
    \cite[def. III.1.1]{Aluffi2009Algebra}.

    \thmitem{rem:ring_unitality/nonunital} Rings are assumed to be nonunital by
    \cite[def. 1.3.1]{Винберг2014КурсАлгебры},
    \cite[152]{Кострикин2020АлгебраЧасть1},
    \cite[271]{Курош1968КурсВысшейАлгебры},
    \cite[112]{Тыртышников2017ОсновыАлгебры} and
    \cite[def. V.1]{ГеновМиховскиМоллов1991Алгебра}.

    \thmitem{rem:ring_unitality/algebras} With respect to algebras, there is much more variety in the definitions.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \Textcite[def. 3.1.1]{Bourbaki1998Algebra1to3} define an \( R \)-algebra \( M \) over a commutative ring as an \( R \)-module with \( R \)-bilinear products, i.e. they assume no multiplicative unit by default. When defining algebra ideals, he explicitly requires ideals to be \( R \)-submodules.

      \Textcite[121]{Lang2002Algebra} uses the same definition, but does not discuss ideals.

      Both assume that rings, but not algebras, are by default unital.

      This definition is closest to ours, however we assume that \( M \) is unital by default.

      \item \Textcite[543]{Rotman2015AdvancedModernAlgebraPart1} and \textcite[4]{КоцевСидеров2016КомутативнаАлгебра} define an \( R \)-algebra \( M \) over a \emph{commutative unital} ring \( R \) as an \( R \)-module that is also a ring satisfying the homogeneity-like condition \eqref{eq:thm:algebra_over_semiring_via_homogeneity} as per \cref{thm:algebra_over_semiring_via_homogeneity}.

      This definition is equivalent to ours in the case of associative unital \( R \)-algebras.

      \Textcite[def. 1.7.1]{Винберг2014КурсАлгебры}, \textcite[66]{Кострикин2020АлгебраЧасть2} and \textcite[def. XI.2]{ГеновМиховскиМоллов1991Алгебра}, who do not require rings to be unital, give similar definitions, but assume that \( R \) is a field.

      Neither specifically discusses algebra ideals.

      \item \Textcite[407]{Jacobson1985BasicAlgebraI} defines a \( \BbbK \)-algebra \( M \) over a field \( \BbbK \) as an \( R \)-module that is also a \emph{unital} ring satisfying \eqref{eq:thm:algebra_over_semiring_via_homogeneity}.

      Ring ideals are then automatically algebra ideals.

      Compared to the authors from the previous bullet, Jacobson only considers unital algebras, but requires the base ring to be a field.

      \item \Textcite[13]{Eisenbud1995CommutativeAlgebra} defines an \( R \)-algebra \( M \) over the commutative unital ring \( R \) via a ring homomorphism \( \varphi: R \to M \) in accordance with \cref{thm:algebra_over_semiring_via_homomorphism}. He requires \( M \) to be commutative.

      The ring ideals in this case are again algebra ideals.

      \Textcite[def. III.5.7]{Aluffi2009Algebra} and \textcite[9]{Kaplansky1974CommutativeRings} and use a similar definition, but allows \( M \) to be noncommutative. Neither explicitly discusses algebra ideals.

      This definition is equivalent to ours in the case of associative unital \( R \)-algebras.
    \end{itemize}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:ring_is_integer_algebra}
  The categories \( \hyperref[def:ring/category]{\cat{Ring}} \) of rings and \( \hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/category]{\cat{Alg}_\BbbZ} \) of integer algebras are \hyperref[rem:category_similarity/isomorphism]{isomorphic}.
\end{proposition}
\begin{comments}
  \item Compare this result to \cref{thm:abelian_group_is_module} for modules and \cref{thm:semiring_is_natural_number_algebra} for algebras over semirings.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  Follows from \cref{thm:semiring_is_natural_number_algebra}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Algebra quotients}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences}
  Fix an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring]{algebra} \( M \). \hyperref[def:fol_congruence]{First order congruences} on \( M \) and \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/ideal]{algebra ideals} of \( M \) are related as follows:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/cong_to_subalgebra} For every congruence in \( M \), the coset \( [0_M] \) of the zero element is a two-sided \( R \)-algebra ideal of \( M \).

    \thmitem{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/subalgebra_to_cong} Conversely, for every two-sided \( R \)-algebra ideal \( I \), the relation
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/subalgebra_to_cong}
      x \cong y \T{if and only if} x - y \in I,
    \end{equation}
    is a congruence.

    \thmitem{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/inverse} The procedures for obtaining a congruence from a two-sided ideal and vice versa are inverses.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{proposition}
\begin{comments}
  \item This allows us to consider the quotient \( M / N \) with respect to an \( R \)-subalgebra rather than \( M / {\cong} \) with respect to a congruence.

  \item This is based on similar statements for \hyperref[def:module]{modules} --- see \cref{thm:submodules_and_congruences}, and for \hyperref[def:ring]{rings} --- see \cref{thm:ring_ideals_and_congruences}.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  \SubProofOf{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/cong_to_subalgebra} Since \( R \)-algebra congruences are \( R \)-module congruences, \cref{thm:submodules_and_congruences} implies that \( [0_M] \) is an \( R \)-submodule of \( M \).

  Similarly, \cref{thm:ring_ideals_and_congruences} (whose proof does not use associativity of \( M \)) implies that \( [0_M] \) is a ring ideal of \( R \).

  It follows that \( [0_M] \) is an algebra ideal of \( R \).

  \SubProofOf{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/subalgebra_to_cong} Follows similarly by using the converses of the analogues for \( R \)-module and ring congruences.

  \SubProofOf{thm:algebra_ideals_and_congruences/inverse} Special case of \cref{thm:normal_subgroups_and_congruences/inverse}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lattice theorem for algebra ideals]\label{thm:lattice_theorem_for_algebra_ideals}
  Given an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/ideal]{algebra ideal} \( I \) of the \( R \)-algebra \( M \), the function \( J \mapsto J / I \) is an \hyperref[def:lattice/homomorphism]{isomorphism of complete lattices} between the \hyperref[def:lattice_ideal/principal]{principal filter} of \( R \)-subalgebras of \( M \) containing \( I \) and the \hyperref[thm:semiring_of_ideals/lattice]{lattice of \( R \)-algebra ideals} of the \hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/quotient]{quotient} \( M / I \).
\end{theorem}
\begin{comments}
  \item See \fullref{thm:lattice_theorem_for_substructures} for a more verbose formulation of this theorem in a general setting.

  \item This is based on a similar statement for \hyperref[def:group/subobject]{subgroups} --- see \fullref{thm:lattice_theorem_for_subgroups}. Another similar statement holds for \hyperref[def:ring/ideal]{ring ideals} --- see \fullref{thm:lattice_theorem_for_ring_ideals} --- and for \hyperref[def:module]{submodules} --- see \fullref{thm:lattice_theorem_for_submodules}.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  Fix an \( R \)-algebra ideal \( I \) of \( M \) and let \( x \cong y \) if \( x - y \) is in \( I \).

  Note that every ideal of \( M \) is a subgroup of (the additive group of) \( M \) and that \( {\cong} \) is also a congruence on the additive group of \( M \). \Fullref{thm:lattice_theorem_for_subgroups} allows us to conclude that the ideal \( J \) of \( M \) contains \( I \) if and only if it is compatible with \( {\cong} \).

  The lattice isomorphism then follows from \fullref{thm:lattice_theorem_for_substructures}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:kernel_is_algebra_ideal}
  The \hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/kernel]{kernel} of an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/morphism]{algebra homomorphism} is an \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/ideal]{algebra ideal} of its domain.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  Follows from \cref{thm:kernel_is_normal_subgroup} and absorption.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Homomorphism theorem for algebras]\label{thm:homomorphism_theorem_for_algebras}
  Every \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/morphism]{algebra homomorphism} \( {\varphi: M \to N} \) induces an isomorphism
  \begin{equation*}
    \begin{aligned}
      &\Phi: M / \ker \varphi \to \img \varphi, \\
      &\Phi([x]) \coloneqq \varphi(x)
    \end{aligned}
  \end{equation*}
  between the \hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/quotient]{quotient algebra} \( M / \ker \varphi \) and the \hyperref[def:set_valued_map/image]{set-theoretic image} \( \img \varphi = \varphi[M] \) of \( \varphi \).
\end{theorem}
\begin{comments}
  \item This is a restatement of \fullref{thm:fol_homomorphism_factorization} that uses ideals rather than congruences.

  It is based on a similar statement for \hyperref[def:group]{groups} --- see \fullref{thm:homomorphism_theorem_for_rings}. Another similar statement holds for \hyperref[def:ring]{rings} --- see \fullref{thm:homomorphism_theorem_for_rings} --- and for \hyperref[def:module]{modules} --- see \fullref{thm:homomorphism_theorem_for_modules}.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  \Fullref{thm:homomorphism_theorem_for_modules} implies that \( \Phi \) is an isomorphism of \( R \)-modules. The fact that it also preserves multiplication follows by the same reasoning as in \fullref{thm:homomorphism_theorem_for_rings}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Algebraic dependence}

\begin{definition}\label{def:algebraic_dependence}\mimprovised
  Fix a \hyperref[def:ring/commutative]{commutative ring} \( R \) and a family \( \seq{ u_k }_{k \in \mscrK} \) from some \( R \)-\hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring]{algebra} \( M \). Consider the \hyperref[def:polynomial_algebra]{polynomial algebra} \( R[X_k \given k \in \mscrK] \).

  We say that the elements of \( \mscrK \) are \term[ru=алгебрически независимые (елементы) (\cite[408]{Винберг2014КурсАлгебры}), en=algebraically independent (elements) (\cite[A.IV.4]{Bourbaki2003Algebra4to7})]{algebraically independent} if any of the following equivalent conditions hold:

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:algebraic_dependence/direct}\mcite[345]{Rotman2015AdvancedModernAlgebraPart1} We have \( f(u_k \given k \in \mscrK) = 0_M \) if and only if \( f \) is the zero polynomial.

    \thmitem{def:algebraic_dependence/evaluation}\mcite[A.IV.4]{Bourbaki2003Algebra4to7} The evaluation map \( \Phi_u: R[X_k \given k \in \mscrK] \to M \) sending \( X_k \) to \( u_k \) is injective.
  \end{thmenum}

  Unsurprisingly, if the elements of \( E \) are not algebraically independent, we say that they are \term[ru=алгебрически зависимые (елементы) (\cite[408]{Винберг2014КурсАлгебры}), en=algebraically dependent (elements) (\cite[A.IV.4]{Bourbaki2003Algebra4to7})]{algebraically dependent}.
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item We have stated this definition so that the similarity with \cref{def:linear_dependence}, defining linear (in)dependence, is clear.

  \Cref{def:algebraic_dependence/direct} is stated for \hyperref[def:field/subobject]{field extensions} in \cite[345]{Rotman2015AdvancedModernAlgebraPart1}.

  \item We are mostly interested in algebraic dependence of polynomials, which can get confusing because polynomials have two separate roles. see \cref{thm:def:algebraic_dependence}.
\end{comments}
\begin{defproof}
  \ImplicationSubProof{def:algebraic_dependence/direct}{def:algebraic_dependence/evaluation} Suppose that \( \seq{ u_k }_{k \in \mscrK} \) is a root only of the zero polynomial. Let \( \Phi_e(f) = \Phi_e(g) \). Then \( \seq{ u_k }_{k \in \mscrK} \) is a root of \( f - g \) and hence the latter is the zero polynomial. But this implies that \( f = g \). Hence, the evaluation map is injective.

  \ImplicationSubProof{def:algebraic_dependence/evaluation}{def:algebraic_dependence/direct} Conversely, suppose that \( \Phi_u \) is injective and that there exists a polynomial \( f(X_k \given k \in \mscrK) \) such that \( \Phi_e(f) = 0_M \).

  For any other polynomial \( g(X_k \given k \in \mscrK) \), we have \( \Phi_u(f g) = 0_M \), and hence either \( f \) is the zero polynomial or the evaluation map is not injective. We have assumed that it is injective, hence \( f \) is the zero polynomial.
\end{defproof}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:def:algebraic_dependence}
  \hyperref[def:algebraic_dependence]{Algebraic (in)dependence} for the \hyperref[def:ring/commutative]{commutative ring} \( R \) has the following basic properties:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/element} Every element of \( R \) is algebraically dependent over \( R \).

    \thmitem{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/n_independent} Different indeterminates are algebraically independent over \( R \).

    \thmitem{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/two_univariate_dependent}\mcite{MathOF:univariate_polynomials_algebraically_dependent} Every two univariate polynomials in \( R \) are algebraically dependent over \( R \).

    \thmitem{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/n_plus_one_dependent} Every \( n + 1 \) polynomials in \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_n] \) are algebraically dependent over \( R \).
  \end{thmenum}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  \SubProofOf{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/element} Every element \( u \) of \( R \) is a root of the nonzero polynomial \( X - u \).

  \SubProofOf{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/n_independent} Fix some indeterminates \( X_1, \ldots, X_n \). For a nonzero polynomial \( f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) \), the substitution \( \Phi_{X_1, \dots, X_n}(f) \) is zero if and only if \( f \) is zero because the evaluation map is the identity.

  \SubProofOf{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/two_univariate_dependent} Fix polynomials \( p(X) \) and \( q(X) \) over \( R \). We will construct a polynomial \( f(Y, Z) \) over \( R \) such that \( \Phi_{p,q}(f) = 0 \).

  If \( p(X) \) is zero, simply define \( f(Y, Z) \coloneqq Z \). If \( q(X) \) is zero, put \( f(Y, Z) \coloneqq Y \).

  Suppose that both are nonzero; denote by \( n \) be the degree of \( p(X) \) and by \( m \) the degree of \( q(X) \). We will consider polynomials of the form \( p^l q^k \).

  Fix a positive integer \( d > 2 \max\set{ n, m } \). We want the degree of \( p^l q^k \) to be at most \( d \). If
  \begin{align*}
    l < \frac d {2n} && k < \frac d {2m},
  \end{align*}
  then, by \cref{thm:univariate_polynomial_product/degree}, either \( p^l q^k \) is the zero polynomial or
  \begin{equation*}
    \deg(p^l q^k) = nl + km < \frac d 2 + \frac d 2 = d.
  \end{equation*}

  These polynomials are all in
  \begin{equation*}
    L_d \coloneqq \lin\set{ 1, X, X^2, X^3, \ldots, X^{d-1} }.
  \end{equation*}

  This is a module of \hyperref[def:module_rank]{rank} \( d \).

  Furthermore, there are \( d^2 / 4nm \) such polynomials. If \( d > 4nm \), there are more polynomials of the form \( p^l q^k \) than the \hyperref[def:module_rank]{rank} of \( L_d \). Hence, every \( d + 1 \) such polynomials are linearly dependent, and hence there exists some nontrivial linear combination
  \begin{equation*}
    a_1 p^{l_1} q^{k_1} + \cdots + a_{d+1} p^{l_{d+1}} q^{k_{d+1}} = 0_{R[X]}.
  \end{equation*}

  We can thus define the following polynomial in \( R[Y, Z] \):
  \begin{equation*}
    f(Y, Z) \coloneqq a_1 Y^{l_1} Z^{k_1} + \cdots + a_{d+1} Y^{l_{d+1}} Z^{k_{d+1}}.
  \end{equation*}

  Then clearly \( \Phi_{p,q}(f) = 0 \), so \( p \) and \( q \) are algebraically dependent over \( R \).

  \SubProofOf{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/n_plus_one_dependent} Let \( p_1, \ldots, p_n, p_{n+1} \) be polynomials in \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1}][X_n] \). By \cref{thm:def:algebraic_dependence/two_univariate_dependent}, the polynomials \( p_n \) and \( p_{n+1} \) are algebraically dependent over \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1}] \).

  Let \( f(Y_n, Y_{n+1}) \) be a polynomial in \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1}][Y_n, Y_{n+1}] \) such that \( \Phi_{p_n,p_{n+1}}(f) = 0 \). The coefficients of \( f \) are themselves polynomials. Let
  \begin{equation*}
    \widehat{f}(Y_1, \ldots, Y_{n-1}, Y_n, Y_{n+1})
  \end{equation*}
  be the polynomial obtained from
  \begin{align*}
    f(X_1, \ldots, X_{n-1}, Y_n, Y_{n+1})
  \end{align*}
  by renaming the corresponding variables.

  Then \( \Phi_{p_1,\ldots,p_{n+1}}(\widehat{f}) = 0 \). Therefore, \( p_1, \ldots, p_{n+1} \) are algebraically dependent over \( R \).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:change_of_polynomial_basis}
  Let \( R \) be a \hyperref[def:ring/commutative]{commutative ring}, let \( X_1, \ldots, X_n \) be arbitrary symbols and consider some polynomials \( Y_k(X_1, \ldots, X_n) \), \( k = 1, \ldots, n \) from \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_n] \) that are \hyperref[def:algebraic_dependence]{algebraically independent}.

  Then the \hyperref[thm:polynomial_algebra_universal_property]{evaluation map} \( \Phi: R[X_1, \ldots, X_n] \to R[X_1, \ldots, X_n][Y_1, \ldots, Y_n] \), given by
  \begin{equation*}
    X_k \mapsto Y_k(X_1, \ldots, X_n),
  \end{equation*}
  is an isomorphic embedding of \( R \)-algebras.
\end{proposition}
\begin{comments}
  \item A polynomial in the image of \( \Phi \) does not explicitly contain any of \( X_1, \ldots, X_n \), hence we can regard it as a polynomial in the indeterminates \( Y_1, \ldots, Y_n \) and then regard \( \Phi \) as an isomorphism between \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_n] \) and \( R[Y_1, \ldots, Y_n] \).

  \item This is a generalization of the \hyperref[con:change_of_basis]{change of basis} of linear operators to polynomials.
\end{comments}
\begin{proof}
  The evaluation map is a homomorphism of \( R \)-algebras, hence it remains only to show that it is injective. Suppose that \( \Phi(f) = \Phi(g) \) for some polynomials \( f \) and \( g \) from \( R[X_1, \ldots, X_n] \).

  Then \( \Phi(f - g) \) is the zero polynomial. Since \( Y_1, \ldots, Y_n \) are algebraically independent, it follows that \( f - g \) is the zero polynomial, that is, \( f = g \).
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Quaternions}

We defined the field \( \BbbC \) of \hyperref[def:complex_numbers]{complex numbers} as the \hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/quotient]{quotient algebra} \( \BbbR[X] / \braket{ X^2 + 1 } \). Furthermore, as discussed in \cref{rem:real_field_extensions}, there are no nontrivial finite-dimensional field extensions of \( \BbbC \).

Nevertheless, we can generalize the construction by instead considering \hyperref[def:noncommutative_polynomial_algebra]{noncommutative polynomials}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:quaternion_algebra}\mimprovised
  We define the \term[bg=кватерниони (\cite[454]{Обрешков1962ВисшаАлгебра}), ru=алгебра кватернионов (\cite[41]{Винберг2014КурсАлгебры}), en=quaternion algebra (\cite[exerc. III.1.12]{Aluffi2009Algebra})]{quaternion algebra} \( \BbbH \) as the \hyperref[def:algebra_over_ring/quotient]{quotient} of the \hyperref[def:noncommutative_polynomial_algebra]{noncommutative polynomial algebra} \( \BbbR\braket{ i, j, k } \) by the congruence \hyperref[def:fol_generated_congruence]{generated} by the following relation:
  \begin{align*}
    i^2 \sim -1, && jk \sim i, && (kj \sim -i), \\
    j^2 \sim -1, && ki \sim j, && (ik \sim -j), \\
    k^2 \sim -1, && ij \sim k, && (ji \sim -k).
  \end{align*}

  \begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[page=1]{output/def__quaternions}
    \caption{An illustration of the congruence in \cref{def:quaternion_algebra} --- by multiplying the head of an arc by its tail, we obtain the third vertex; by multiplying the tail by the head, we instead obtain the negation.}\label{fig:def:quaternion_algebra}
  \end{figure}

  Since every possible pair of symbols can now be reduced to a single one, we are left with only four monomials, and we can denote a quaternion as a (real) linear combination:
  \begin{equation*}
    a + bi + cj + dk.
  \end{equation*}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item The last column defining the congruence is redundant because
  \begin{equation*}
    kj \sim (-1)^2 kj \sim (-1) j^2 (kj) \sim -j (jk) j \sim -j (ij) \sim -jk \sim -i
  \end{equation*}
  and analogously for the others.

  \item The letter \( \BbbH \) is chosen in honor of William Hamilton, who introduced them in a multi-part paper over the course of several years, from 1846 to 1850. The compiled paper can be found in \cite{Hamilton2000Quaternions}.
\end{comments}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:def:quaternion_algebra}
  \hyperref[def:quaternion_algebra]{Quaternions} have the following basic properties:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:def:quaternion_algebra/commutative} Multiplication of quaternions is not commutative.
    \thmitem{thm:def:quaternion_algebra/inverse} \( \BbbH \) is a \hyperref[def:division_ring]{division ring} --- every nonzero quaternion \( a + bi + cj + dk \) has a (two-sided) multiplicative inverse:
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:thm:def:quaternion_algebra/inverse}
      \frac {a - bi - cj - dk} {a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2}
    \end{equation}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  \SubProofOf{thm:def:quaternion_algebra/commutative} We have \( ij = k = -ji \).
  \SubProofOf{thm:def:quaternion_algebra/inverse} We have
  \begin{align*}
    &\phantom{{}={}}
    (a + bi + cj + dk) (a - bi - cj - dk)
    = \\ &=
    [\underline{a^2} - (ab)i - (ac)j - (ad)k]
    +
    [(ab)i + \underline{b^2} - (bc)k + (bd)j]
    + \\ &+
    [(ac)j + (bc)k + \underline{c^2} - (cd)i]
    +
    [(ad)k - (bd)j + (cd)i + \underline{d^2}]
    = \\ &=
    a^2 + b^2 + c^2 + d^2.
  \end{align*}

  Furthermore,
  \begin{align*}
    &\phantom{{}={}}
    (a - bi - cj - dk) (a + bi + cj + dk)
    = \\ &=
    [a + (-b) i + (-c) j + (-d) k] [a - (-b) i - (-c) j - (-d) k]
    = \\ &=
    a^2 + (-b)^2 + (-c)^2 + (-d)^2.
  \end{align*}
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}\label{def:quaternionic_group}\mcite[128]{Aluffi2009Algebra}
  We also define the \term{quaternionic group} \( Q_8 \) as the multiplicative subgroup \( \braket{ 1, i, j, k } \) of the \hyperref[def:quaternion_algebra]{quaternion algebra} \( \BbbH \) consisting of the four basis elements and their inverses.
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item It is tempting to define the quaternion algebra as the group algebra of \( Q_8 \), but that would make the algebra eight-dimensional over \( \BbbR \), with distinct coordinates for \( i \) and \( -i \), \( j \) and \( -j \) and so forth.
\end{comments}

\begin{example}\label{ex:quaternion_polynomial_with_infinitely_many_roots}
  Consider the \hyperref[def:noncommutative_polynomial_algebra]{noncommutative polynomial} \( f(X) = X^2 + 1 \) over the \hyperref[def:quaternion_algebra]{quaternion algebra} \( \BbbH \).

  The square of the quaternion \( x \coloneqq bi + cj + dk \) is
  \begin{align*}
    &\phantom{{}={}}
    (bi + cj + dk) (bi + cj + dk)
    = \\ &=
    [-\underline{b^2} + (bc)k - (bd)j]
    + \\ &+
    [-(bc)k - \underline{c^2} + (cd)i]
    +
    [(bd)j - (cd)i - \underline{d^2}]
    = \\ &=
    -b^2 - c^2 - d^2.
  \end{align*}

  Therefore, if \( b^2 + c^2 + d^2 = 1 \), then \( x^2 = -1 \), and \( f(x) = 0 \). If we extend \cref{def:polynomial_root} of polynomial roots to noncommutative polynomials, we obtain that \( f(X) \) has infinitely many roots.

  This contrasts with the commutative case, where \cref{thm:polynomial_degree_bounds_root_count} implies that \hyperref[def:entire_semiring]{entire} rings have at most \( n \) roots for a polynomial of degree \( n \).
\end{example}
