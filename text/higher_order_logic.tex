\section{Higher-order logic}\label{sec:higher_order_logic}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:higher_order_logic_and_type_theory}
  Type theory allows two unrelated approaches to \hyperref[con:predicate_logic]{predicate logic}:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{rem:higher_order_logic_and_type_theory/terms} As mentioned in \cref{rem:type_theory}, Alonzo Church initially introduced typed \( \muplambda \)-calculus as a way to encode logical formulas via \hyperref[con:type_annotation]{type-annotated} \hyperref[def:lambda_term]{\( \muplambda \)-terms}.

    This approach was later extended by his students Leon Henkin and Peter Andrews. Andrews describes a \hyperref[con:logical_system]{logical system} \( \logic{Q}_0 \) in \cite[ch. 5]{Andrews2002Logic} based on his and Henkin's refinements. More recently, William Farmer suggests modernized variants of Church's and Andrews' systems; he briefly outlines such a system in \cite{Farmer2008STTVirtues} and \cite{Farmer1990PartialFunctionSTT}. All these systems presuppose \hyperref[con:classical_logic]{classical logic}.

    Based on the availability of \fullref{ch:lambda_calculus}, which itself grew out of Church's system, we provide a further refinement of their formulations. It is in principle not limited to classical logic, but we will not purpose such generalizations.

    Because of the variety of influences involved, we comment the origin of each definition individually. The relations of our system and those mentioned above are discussed in \cref{rem:hol_notational_shorthands}, \cref{rem:hol_formula_abbreviations} and \cref{rem:hol_axiomatic_derivations}.

    \thmitem{rem:higher_order_logic_and_type_theory/types} A more powerful approach is based on the \hyperref[con:curry_howard_correspondence]{Curry-Howard correspondence}, which hints at how \hi{types} rather than \( \muplambda \)-terms can be used to encode logical formulas.

    The correspondence itself is briefly outlined in \cref{con:curry_howard_correspondence} and formalized for \hyperref[def:propositional_formula]{propositional formulas} via \fullref{alg:type_derivation_to_proof_tree} and \fullref{alg:proof_tree_to_type_derivation}.

    We can use a \hyperref[con:syntax_fragment]{fragment} of \hyperref[def:martin_lof_type_theory]{Martin-L\"of type theory} to emulate Church's original system. We further discuss this possibility in \cref{rem:mltt_hol}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Syntax of higher-order logic}

\begin{definition}\label{def:quantifiable_type}\mimprovised
  As discussed in \cref{con:simple_type_theory/hol}, Church uses restricted \hyperref[def:simple_type]{simple types} in \cite{Church1940STT} for his formulation of higher-order logic. We will call them \term{quantifiable types} to distinguish them from other simple types we consider in this monograph.

  Fix a nonempty finite set \( \op*{Sort} \), whose elements act as \hyperref[def:simple_type]{base types}. Unsurprisingly, we will call them \term{sorts}. Unless explicitly noted otherwise, we presuppose a single sort --- the \term[en=type of individuals (\cite[56]{Church1940STT})]{type of individuals} \( \syn\iota \)\fnote{The term \enquote{individuals} is based on Russell's usage; see \cref{rem:hol_formula_order_origin}. The symbol we use to denote their type is Church's small iota from \cite[56]{Church1940STT} with a dot on top, as per \cref{rem:mathematical_logic_conventions/terminal_dots}.}.

  We also presuppose another base type --- the \term[en=type of propositions (\cite[56]{Church1940STT})]{type of propositions} \( \syn\omicron \)\fnote{\tcite{In #2, where #1}[56]{Church1940STT} pioneers a dedicated syntax for types, formulas have a dedicated type --- the \enquote{type of propositions}, denoted by \( \omicron \). It can be regarded as either a Latin or a Greek letter; here we suppose the latter. In accordance with \cref{rem:mathematical_logic_conventions/terminal_dots}, we place a dot on top.} --- that we do not consider a sort.

  The quantifiable types are then the aforementioned base types and the arrow types generated by them (without type variables, product and sum types).
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item In practice, quantifiable types depend on a \hyperref[def:hol_signature]{signature of higher-order logic}.

  \item The sorts must not include improper symbols from the \hyperref[def:simple_type_alphabet]{alphabet of simple types}, but are allowed to range over small Greek identifiers because there are no type variables to clash with. In fact, both the type of individuals and the type of propositions are small Greek identifiers.

  \item Quantifiable types are what Church originally calls \enquote{simple types}, but generalized to more than one sort.

  \item We decided to call the non-propositional base types \enquote{sorts} based on similar usage in many-sorted first-order logic, which is discussed in \cite[\S 4.4.13]{TroelstraSchwichtenberg2000BasicProofTheory} and \cite[def. 62]{GoguenBurstall1992Institutions}.

  Sorts, as defined here, are unrelated to sorts in \hyperref[def:pure_type_system]{pure type systems}, which act as \hyperref[con:type_universe]{type universes} rather than types.
\end{comments}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_signature}\mimprovised
  A \hyperref[con:logical_system_signature]{signature} for higher-order logic consists of the following:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:hol_signature/sorts} A nonempty \hyperref[def:formal_language/alphabet]{alphabet} \( \op*{Sort} \), whose elements act as sorts of \hyperref[def:quantifiable_type]{quantifiable types}.

    These sorts must not include improper symbols from the \hyperref[def:simple_type_alphabet]{alphabet of simple types} and the \hyperref[def:inference_rule_alphabet]{alphabet of inference rules}, but are allowed to range over Greek identifiers (because we discard type variables).

    We call the resulting logical system \term[en=one-sorted (predicate calculus) (\cite[84]{Kleene2002Logic})]{one-sorted} or \term[en=many-sorted predicate logic (\cite[\S 4.4.13]{TroelstraSchwichtenberg2000BasicProofTheory})]{many-sorted} depending on the amount of sorts.

    \thmitem{def:hol_signature/nl_const} A possibly empty alphabet \( \op*{NLConst} \), whose elements act as \hyperref[def:lambda_term]{constant \( \muplambda \)-terms}. We call them \term{nonlogical constants}\fnote{We stated in \cref{rem:logical_symbol_terminology} that the separation into \enquote{logical} and \enquote{nonlogical} symbols is generally ambiguous, but in this case there will be no ambiguity because we specify the kind of entity we refer to, e.g. \enquote{nonlogical constant} rather than simply \enquote{nonlogical symbol}.}.

    We disallow the improper symbols from the \hyperref[def:lambda_term_alphabet]{alphabet of \( \muplambda \)-calculus} and the \hyperref[def:inference_rule_alphabet]{alphabet of inference rules}, as well as all Latin identifiers. We also disallow the logical constants from \cref{def:hol_typing_rules}.

    \thmitem{def:hol_signature/nl_type} A set \( \op*{NLType} \), whose elements are \hyperref[def:type_assertion]{type assertions} for the nonlogical constants. We require exactly one assertion per nonlogical constant.

    For each nonlogical constant \( c \), then, if \( c: \tau \) is its assertion, we introduce the \hyperref[def:simple_typing_rule]{simple typing rule}
    \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{Ax}_c }]{inf:def:hol_signature/nl_type}
      \begin{prooftree}
        \infer0[\ref{inf:def:hol_signature/nl_type}]{ c: \tau }.
      \end{prooftree}
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item We discuss some cardinality considerations in \cref{rem:language_alphabet_cardinality}; we mention here only that both alphabets are by default assumed to be finite. In accordance with \cref{rem:cardinality_auxiliary_terminology}, we use cardinality-related terminology like \enquote{finite} and \enquote{countably infinite} for the signature itself when we mean the larger of the cardinalities of the alphabets.

  \item Explicit signatures are not considered by Church nor Henkin. \incite*[\S 55]{Andrews2002Logic} distinguishes between different \enquote{languages} of his system \( \logic{Q}_0 \), where a single language consists of all well-formed formulas. So Andrews also does not consider signatures, but only the resulting sets of formulas.

  Our definition is instead (loosely) based on \bycite[270]{Farmer2008STTVirtues}:
  \begin{displayquote}
    A \textit{language} of STT is a pair \( L = (\mscrC, \tau) \) where is \( \mscrC \) is a set of symbols called \textit{constants} and \( \tau: \mscrC \to \mscrT \) is a total function. That is, a language is a set of symbols with assigned types (what computer scientists usually call a \enquote{signature}). The constants are the nonlogical primitive symbols that are used to construct the expressions of the language.
  \end{displayquote}

  Farmer assumes here that \( \syn\iota \) is the only sort, but later suggests adding other sorts (which he calls base types).
\end{comments}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hol_notational_shorthands}
  We will use several notational shorthands for higher-order logic that emulate Church's notation from \cite{Church1940STT} (or rather Farmer's refinement from \cite{Farmer2008STTVirtues}):

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/variable_annotations} There is a discrepancy between Church's theory of types from \cite{Church1940STT} and modernized simple type theory as presented in \fullref{sec:simply_typed_lambda_terms}.

    Namely, as discussed in \cref{rem:typing_style}, Church attaches type annotations to every \( \muplambda \)-term. For convenience, he supposes that every type has a distinct set of variables. This is in contrast with the modernized simple type theory presented in \fullref{sec:simply_typed_lambda_terms}, where only quantifier variables are annotated.

    Namely, the Church-style term
    \begin{equation*}
      (f^{\tau \synimplies \sigma} x^\tau)^\sigma
    \end{equation*}
    consists of two variables of different types\fnote{Church placed the types as subscripts rather than as superscripts; see \cref{rem:type_theory}.}. The variables here are distinguished on the level of syntax.

    In contrast, in a modern theory we have the inference
    \begin{equation*}
      \begin{prooftree}
        \hypo{ f: \tau \synimplies \sigma }
        \hypo{ x: \tau }
        \infer2[\ref{inf:def:arrow_type/elim}]{ f x: \sigma }
      \end{prooftree}
    \end{equation*}

    Here \( f \) and \( x \) are both untyped variables that are parts of type assertions. As long as we do not care about the inference tree, we can express the above as the sequent
    \begin{equation*}
      (f: \tau \synimplies \sigma, x: \tau) \vdash f x: \sigma.
    \end{equation*}

    In \cref{def:hol_expression}, we define an expression of higher-order logic as a triple \( (T, M, \tau) \), where \( T \vdash M: \tau \) and all variables of \( T \) are free in \( M \). \Cref{thm:hol_expression_type_uniqueness} shows that, as long as \( M \) is \hyperref[def:typability]{typable} in \( T \), \( \tau \) is uniquely determined by \( T \) and \( M \). This justifies visually attaching a type to each free variable (somewhat resembling Church's notation):
    \begin{equation*}
      f^{\tau \synimplies \sigma} x^\tau.
    \end{equation*}

    A similar approach is used by \incite[def. 2A7]{Hindley1997BasicSTT}.

    \thmitem{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/arrow_types} Another case where we deviate from Church's notation on purpose is arrow types --- we denote by \( \tau \synimplies \sigma \) what Church denotes by \( \sigma\tau \).

    As discussed in \cref{rem:arrow_type_name}, the arrow notation is preferred by modern authors.

    \thmitem{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/placeholder_annotations} Similarly, we have defined typed \( \muplambda \)-term \hyperref[con:schemas_and_instances]{schemas} in \cref{def:lambda_term_schema} so that neither variable schemas nor placeholders have type annotations attached.

    Since there are no type contexts for schemas, in the object language we instead place type annotations on all variables and placeholders, and disallow the same variable or placeholder with different annotations.

    For example, the schema \( F^{\tau \synimplies \sigma} x^\tau \) designates the types of the placeholder \( F \) and free variable \( x \). For bound variables, we avoid repeating the type annotation as a metalingual convention. So we write \( \qabs {x^\tau} x^\tau \) as \( \qabs {x^\tau} x \).

    Another convention we will use is to denote formula placeholders --- those of type \( \syn\omicron \) --- via letters like \( \varphi \), \( \psi \) and \( \theta \) (without superscripts), in accordance with \cref{rem:mathematical_logic_conventions/greek_alphabet}.

    \thmitem{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/abbreviations} In order to express logical constructs, Church suggests starting with several constant \( \muplambda \)-terms and \hyperref[con:syntactic_abbreviation]{abbreviating} other \( \muplambda \)-terms to obtain a more familiar logical syntax.

    There are thus two languages involved --- the familiar syntax of simply typed \( \muplambda \)-terms, as well as a dedicated syntax for predicate logic. These are appropriately viewed as distinct layers rather than alternatives, since both the syntax and semantics of the logical formulas depends on the underlying \( \muplambda \)-terms, while the usability of the system as an \hyperref[def:general_logic]{general logic} relies on the upper \enquote{logical} layer.

    We describe in \cref{rem:hol_formula_abbreviations} how different authors offer different abbreviations leading to the same logical layer. These abbreviations depend on classical propositional equivalences like those in \cref{thm:classical_equivalences}.

    We prefer a more straightforward approach, where each connective and quantifier has its own dedicated constant --- see \cref{def:hol_typing_rules}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_expression}\mimprovised
  Over a fixed \hyperref[def:hol_signature]{signature} \( \Sigma \) of higher-order logic, suppose that the \( \muplambda \)-term \( M \) is \hyperref[def:typability]{typable} in the \hyperref[def:type_context]{type context} \( T \) with respect to the arrow typing rules \ref{inf:def:arrow_type/elim} and \ref{inf:def:arrow_type/intro/explicit}, the family of constant typing rules \ref{inf:def:hol_signature/nl_type}, and the formula formation that we will discuss in \cref{def:hol_typing_rules}.

  Based on our discussion in \cref{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/variable_annotations}, we call the triple \( (T, M, \tau) \) a \term{well-formed expression} of higher-order logic if all variables in \( T \) are free in \( M \). \Cref{thm:hol_expression_type_uniqueness} shows that \( \tau \) is uniquely determined by \( M \) and \( T \).

  As per \cref{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/placeholder_annotations}, we annotate metalingual placeholders for expressions, so \( M^\tau \) denotes an expression of type \( \tau \).

  We denote the set of all expressions over \( \Sigma \) by \( \op*{Expr}_\Sigma \).

  \begin{thmenum}[resume=def:hol_expression]
    \thmitem{def:hol_expression/formula} If \( \tau \) is the type of propositions, we also call the expression a \term{formula}.

    As per \cref{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/placeholder_annotations}, we use the familiar symbols like \( \varphi \), \( \psi \) and \( \theta \) (without superscripts) for denoting formulas. \Cref{def:hol_typing_rules} lists formation rules for formulas.

    We denote the set of all formulas over \( \Sigma \) by \( \op*{Form}_\Sigma \).

    \thmitem{def:hol_expression/predicate} If \( \tau = \sigma_1 \synimplies \cdots \synimplies \sigma_n \synimplies \syn\omicron \), we instead call the expression a \term[en=predicate (\cite[270]{Farmer2008STTVirtues})]{predicate}.

    Predicates may be full-fledged syntactic statements, but may also be variables annotated with an appropriate type.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item Our usage of the phrase \enquote{well-formed expression} here is aligned with the informal general usage described in \cref{con:expression}.

  \begin{itemize}
    \item \incite{Church1940STT} uses \enquote{well-formed formula}, as does \incite[211]{Andrews2002Logic}. We want to generalize the terminology of first-order logic, so we only use \enquote{formula} for expressions inhabiting type of propositions.

    \item \incite[270]{Farmer2008STTVirtues} uses \enquote{expression}, which we find too general given that we allow expressions to be ill-formed.

    \item Since the crucial component of an expression is a \( \muplambda \)-term, to avoid confusion we refrain from using \enquote{term} for anything else (even though it seems fitting to generalize first-order terms).
  \end{itemize}
\end{comments}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_closed_expression}
  As in the case of ordinary \hyperref[def:lambda_term]{\( \muplambda \)-terms}, if a \hyperref[def:hol_expression]{higher-order logic expression} has no \hyperref[def:lambda_variable_freeness]{free variables}, we say that it is \term[en=closed expression (\cite[270]{Farmer2008STTVirtues})]{closed}.

  As in \hyperref[def:first_order_logic]{first-order logic}, we refer to closed formulas as \term{sentences}\fnote{We will not call them \enquote{combinators} even though they technically are uniquely determined by combinators.}.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:quantifiable_type_uncurrying}
  For \hyperref[def:hol_expression]{higher-order expressions}, we will find it convenient to utilize, within the \hyperref[con:metalogic]{metalanguage}, the different function application notations from \cref{def:function_application_notation}.

  For the term \( M^\tau \), where \( \tau = \sigma_1 \synimplies \cdots \synimplies \sigma_n \synimplies \theta \) and \( \theta \) is atomic, we will find it convenient to write \( M(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \) rather than the conventional \( \muplambda \)-term application syntax \( M x_1 \ldots x_n \), which itself is a simplification of the notation with explicit parentheses, \( (\ldots ((M x_1) x_2) \ldots x_n) \). This acts as \hyperref[def:function_currying]{uncurrying} on the level of syntax.

  Of course, most constants we will encounter will be non-letter symbols denoting binary operations, and for them we will prefer infix notation.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_typing_rules}
  In higher-order logic, we presuppose several \hyperref[def:lambda_term]{constant \( \muplambda \)-terms}, which we call \term{logical constants}, as well as associated \hyperref[def:simple_typing_rule]{simple typing rules}.

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:hol_typing_rules/prop} The simplest are the rules emulating propositional logic. These are based on the constants \( \synH_\top \), \( \synH_\bot \), \( \synH_\neg \), as well as \( \synH_{\syncirc} \) for every \hyperref[def:propositional_alphabet/connectives]{propositional connective} \( {\syncirc} \):
    \columnratio{0.2,0.2,0.2,0.3}
    \begin{paracol}{4}
      \begin{nthcolumn}{0}
        \ParacolAlignmentHack
        \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_\top }]{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/top}
          \begin{prooftree}
            \infer0[\ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/top}]{ \synH_\top: \syn\omicron }
          \end{prooftree}
        \end{equation*}
      \end{nthcolumn}

      \begin{nthcolumn}{1}
        \ParacolAlignmentHack
        \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_\bot }]{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/bot}
          \begin{prooftree}
            \infer0[\ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/bot}]{ \synH_\bot: \syn\omicron }
          \end{prooftree}
        \end{equation*}
      \end{nthcolumn}

      \begin{nthcolumn}{2}
        \ParacolAlignmentHack
        \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_\neg }]{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/neg}
          \begin{prooftree}
            \infer0[\ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/neg}]{ \synH_\neg: \syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron }
          \end{prooftree}
        \end{equation*}
      \end{nthcolumn}

      \begin{nthcolumn}{3}
        \ParacolAlignmentHack
        \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_{\syncirc} }]{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/conn}
          \begin{prooftree}
            \infer0[\ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/conn}]{ \synH_{\syncirc}: \syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron }
          \end{prooftree}
        \end{equation*}
      \end{nthcolumn}
    \end{paracol}

    We will use \hyperref[con:syntactic_abbreviation]{metalingual abbreviations} to emulate the familiar syntax of predicate logic. For example, we will abbreviate \( \synH_\top \) as \( \syntop \) and \( \synH_\neg \varphi \) as \( \synneg \varphi \), but we will retain \( \synH_\neg \) as a standalone expression.

    \thmitem{def:hol_typing_rules/equality} For equality, we will need a constant \( \synH_= \) and the rule
    \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_= }]{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/eq}
      \begin{prooftree}
        \hypo{ \synM: \syn\tau }
        \hypo{ \synN: \syn\tau }
        \infer2[\ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/eq}]{ \synH_= \synM \synN: \syn\omicron }
      \end{prooftree}
    \end{equation*}

    We abbreviate \( \synH_= \synM \synN \) as \( \synM^\syn\tau \syneq \synN^\syn\tau \).

    Note that, unlike in \ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/prop/conn}, \( \synH_= \) is not by itself typable. We could assign it a type like \( \synH_=:\syn\tau \synimplies \syn\tau \synimplies \syn\omicron \), but then \( \synH_= \) would inhabit more than one type, which would break \cref{thm:hol_expression_type_uniqueness}.

    \thmitem{def:hol_typing_rules/quant} Finally, for every quantifier \( Q \), we need the constant \( \synH_Q \) and the rule
    \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_Q }]{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/quant}
      \begin{prooftree}
        \hypo{ \synx: \syn\tau }
        \hypo{ \syn\varphi: \syn\omicron }
        \infer2[\ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/quant}]{ \synH_Q (\qabs {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \syn\varphi): \syn\omicron }
      \end{prooftree}
    \end{equation*}

    We rely on all typable terms involving \( \synH_Q \) to have an abstraction as an argument, which allows us to abbreviate \( \synH_Q (\qabs {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \syn\varphi) \) as \( \quantifier Q {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \syn\varphi \).
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item As discussed in \cref{rem:hol_notational_shorthands/abbreviations}, unlike Church and his students Henkin and Andrews, we do not pursue minimalism, so we add a dedicated constant for every propositional connective.

  \item We use the symbol \( \synimplies \) both as a connective for arrow types and as a connective for formulas.
\end{comments}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:hol_expression_type_uniqueness}
  The type of a \hyperref[def:hol_expression]{higher-order logic expression} is unique: for every expression \( (T, M, \tau) \), if \( T \vdash M: \sigma \), then \( \sigma = \tau \).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  We must extend the recursion in the proof of \cref{thm:typed_term_habitation_uniqueness} to handle the new typing rules from \cref{def:hol_typing_rules}, as well as \ref{inf:def:hol_signature/nl_type}. The actual recursive cases are straightforward.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hol_formula_abbreviations}
  The choice of \hyperref[con:primitive_notion]{primitive} constant \( \muplambda \)-terms, used to encode logical constructs, has evolved a lot since the publication of \bycite{Church1940STT}.

  Church originally used several constants --- \( N_{oo} \) for \hyperref[def:propositional_alphabet/negation]{negation}, \( A_{ooo} \) for \hyperref[def:propositional_alphabet/connectives/conjunction]{conjunction}, \( \Pi_{o(o\alpha)} \) for \hyperref[def:predicate_logic_alphabet/quantifiers/universal]{universal quantification} and \( \iota_{\alpha(o\alpha)} \) for \hyperref[con:description_operator/iota]{definite description}. The latter two are in fact families of constants depending on \( \alpha \).

  Church's student \incite{Henkin1950CompletenessInTheoryOfTypes} reused Church's constants, but later in \incite*{Henkin1963TheoryOfPropositionalTypes} he presented a formulation that used only \( Q_{o \alpha \alpha } \) for \hyperref[def:predicate_logic_alphabet/equality]{equality}, acknowledging that one such constant is needed for each type \( \alpha \). This latter formulation was restricted to propositional formulas, but later in \cite{Henkin1975Identity} Henkin extended it to all types supported by Church's original system.

  We show concrete abbreviations in \cref{tab:rem:hol_formula_abbreviations}, based on the constant \( \synH_{\syneq} \), typable via the rule \ref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules/eq}. These abbreviations depend on classical propositional equivalences like those in \cref{thm:classical_equivalences}.

  Most of these abbreviations are based on \bycite[212]{Andrews2002Logic}, with the exception of \( \syntop \), which is instead taken from \bycite[273]{Farmer2008STTVirtues}. In the abbreviation of \( \varphi \synwedge \psi \), as in \cref{def:atomic_lambda_term_substitution/sharp}, the function \( \sharp(V) \) gives the smallest identifier not in \( V \).

        \( \varphi \synwedge \psi \)     & \( (\qabs {f^{\syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron}} f \syntop \syntop) \syneq (\qabs {f^{\syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron \synimplies \syn\omicron}} f \varphi \psi) \), \\
                                         & \quad where \( f = \sharp(\op*{Free}(\varphi) \cup \op*{Free}(\psi)) \)                                                                                                                                  \\
        \( \varphi \synvee \psi \)       & \( \synneg (\synneg \varphi \synwedge \synneg \psi) \)                                                                                                                                                   \\
        \( \varphi \synimplies \psi \)   & \( \varphi \syneq \varphi \synwedge \psi \)                                                                                                                                                              \\
        \( \varphi \syniff \psi \)       & \( \varphi \syneq \psi \)                                                                                                                                                                                \\
        \( \qforall {x^\tau} \varphi \)  & \( (\qabs {x^\tau} \varphi) \syneq (\qabs {x^\tau} \syntop) \)                                                                                                                                           \\
        \( \qexists {x^\tau} \varphi \)  & \( \synneg (\qforall {x^\tau} \synneg \varphi) \)                                                                                                                                                        \\
        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \caption{Encoding \hyperref[con:predicate_logic]{predicate logic} in \hyperref[def:higher_order_logic]{simply typed higher-order logic}.}\label{tab:rem:hol_formula_abbreviations}
  \end{table}

  \incite*[\S 51]{Andrews2002Logic} presents a refinement of the systems of Church, Henkin and himself. Andrews calls this system \( \logic{Q}_0 \). It is based on two families of primitive constant \( \muplambda \)-terms --- \( Q_{o \alpha \alpha} \) for formal equality and \( \iota_{\iota (o \iota)} \) for definite description. \incite{Farmer2008STTVirtues} instead uses dedicated syntax for equality and definite descriptions.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:mltt_hol}
  We mentioned in \cref{rem:mltt_curry_howard/arrow_types} that \hyperref[def:martin_lof_type_theory]{Martin-L\"of type theory} can be used as an alternative syntax for higher-order logic.

  This would require us to consider two \hyperref[def:mltt_signature/universe]{type universes} --- \( \syn\BbbQ \) for quantifiable types and \( \syn\omicron \) for formulas, and to restrict the types of bound variables to \( \syn\BbbQ \).

  Several subtleties arise (all of which are easily fixable):
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{rem:mltt_hol/arrows} The arrow type \( \varphi \synimplies \psi \) inhabiting \( \syn\iota \) is an abbreviation for \( \qprod {x^\varphi} \psi \), where \( \psi \) does not depend on \( x \). We cannot require \( \varphi \) to be a quantifiable type in this situation since it must be a formula.

    One way out of this situation is to regard \( {\synimplies} \) (and also \( {\syntimes} \) and \( {\synplus} \)) as dedicated syntactic entries (as in simple type theory) rather than as abbreviations of dependent products or sums.

    \thmitem{rem:mltt_hol/judgmental_equality} We consider dependent types up to \hyperref[con:equality]{judgmental equality} (encompassing \hyperref[def:beta_eta_reduction]{\( \beta\eta \)-equivalence}), while in logic we regard \( \qforall {x^\tau} \varphi \) and \( \qforall {y^\tau} \varphi[y \mapsto x] \) as distinct.

    So, we must disregard implicit judgmental equality.

    \thmitem{rem:mltt_hol/rules} Some \hyperref[con:typing_rule]{typing rules} like \ref{inf:def:dependent_product/intro} transfer directly to their counterpart \ref{inf:def:hol_quantifier_rules/eigenvariables/forall_intro}, while other like \ref{inf:def:dependent_sum/elim} differ from \ref{inf:def:hol_quantifier_rules/eigenvariables/exists_elim} in subtle ways --- see \cref{rem:dependent_products_and_forall_quantifier_rules}.

    Finally, some logical rules like those in \cref{def:hol_equality_rules} have no type-theoretic counterpart.

    This requires us to state new rules rather than reuse those from \fullref{sec:dependent_types}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Henkin semantics}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:henkin_semantics}
  The general construction of \hyperref[def:fol_term_model]{term models} for \fullref{thm:fol_model_existence_theorem} is due to Henkin, as discussed in \cref{rem:henkin_theories}. For this reason, certain authors like \cite[xii]{Hinman2005Logic} refer to them as \enquote{Henkin models}.

  \incite*{Henkin1950CompletenessInTheoryOfTypes} generalizes \hyperref[def:fol_structure]{first-order structures} to \hyperref[def:higher_order_logic]{higher-order logic} and calls them \enquote{standard models} and \enquote{general models}. In accordance with \cref{rem:model_theory_structure_terminology}, in \cref{def:hol_structure} we call these generalizations \term{structures} rather than \enquote{models}.

  This leads to two completely different notions that can be referred to as \enquote{Henkin models} across the literature. We will try to be unambiguous.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_frame}\mimprovised
  A \term[en=frame (\cite[238]{Andrews2002Logic})]{frame} over a \hyperref[def:hol_signature]{signature} \( \Sigma \) of \hyperref[def:higher_order_logic]{higher-order logic} is a \hyperref[def:indexed_family]{family} \( X = \seq{ X_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} \) of sets, indexed by the set \( \BbbQ_\Sigma \) of all \hyperref[def:quantifiable_type]{quantifiable types} over \( \Sigma \), and subject to some constraints. We call \( X_\tau \) the \term{universe} or \term{domain} of type \( \tau \).

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:hol_frame/sorts} For every sort \( S \), the universe \( X_S \) is intended to be chosen freely.

    However, by default we require \( X_S \) to be nonempty. See \cref{rem:hol_empty_universe/semantics} for a broader discussion.

    \thmitem{def:hol_frame/propositions} For the type of propositions \( \syn\omicron \), we require that \( X_{\syn\omicron} \) is a \hyperref[def:truth_value_algebra]{truth value algebra}.

    Unless explicitly noted otherwise, we presuppose that \( X_{\syn\omicron} \) is the classical two-valued Boolean algebra \( \set{ \semtop, \sembot } \).

    \thmitem{def:hol_frame/functions} For every arrow type \( \tau \synimplies \sigma \), we require \( X_{\tau \synimplies \sigma} \) to be a set of functions from \( X_\tau \) to \( X_\sigma \) (also assumed nonempty by default).

    We call the frame \term{standard} if, for every arrow type \( \tau \synimplies \sigma \), the universe \( X_{\tau \synimplies \sigma} \) contains \hi{all} functions from \( X_\tau \) to \( X_\sigma \). If the frame is \hi{not necessarily} standard, we say that it is \term{general}.
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item In a standard frame, it is sufficient to specify only the universes of sorts.

  \item We adapt Andrew's definition from \cite[238]{Andrews2002Logic} to many-sorted signatures. Unlike Andrews, we refer to the individual sets as \enquote{universes} rather than \enquote{domains}; see \cref{rem:model_theory_universe_terminology}.

  Additionally, we distinguish between general and standard frames, while Andrews (and Henkin) only distinguish between general and standard \hyperref[def:hol_structure]{structures}.
\end{comments}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_structure}\mimprovised
  A \term{structure} \( \mscrX = (X, I) \) over a \hyperref[def:hol_signature]{signature} \( \Sigma \) of higher-order logic consists of the following:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{def:hol_structure/frame} A \hyperref[def:hol_frame]{frame} \( X = \seq{ X_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} \) over \( \Sigma \).

    We refer to the structure as \term[en=general model (\cite[84]{Henkin1950CompletenessInTheoryOfTypes})]{general} or \term[en=standard model (\cite[83]{Henkin1950CompletenessInTheoryOfTypes})]{standard} depending on whether \( X \) is.

    \thmitem{def:hol_structure/interpretation} A map \( I \), called an \term{interpretation}, that sends each \hyperref[def:hol_signature/nl_const]{non-logical constant} \( c \) of type \( \tau \) to \( X_\tau \).
  \end{thmenum}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item The interpretation is well-defined as a function since it maps \( \op*{NLConst}_\Sigma \) to the set-theoretic union \( \bigcup_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} X_\tau \).

  \item We will use matching letters used for the frame and for the structure itself, i.e. \( X \) and \( \mscrX \) or \( Y \) and \( \mscrY \). This is discussed in \cref{rem:fol_structure_notation}.

  \item This definition generalizes structures as defined for first-order logic in \cref{def:fol_structure}. It is roughly based on the one by Henkin from \cite[83]{Henkin1950CompletenessInTheoryOfTypes}.

  \incite[238]{Andrews2002Logic} instead calls an \enquote{interpretation} what we call a structure, while \incite[271]{Farmer2008STTVirtues} calls it a \enquote{model}, irrespective of whether a logical theory is involved. We generally avoid calling arbitrary structures \enquote{models} without logical theories involved; see \cref{rem:model_theory_structure_terminology}.
\end{comments}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hol_empty_universe/semantics}
  We discussed in \cref{rem:fol_empty_universe/semantics} how, over a \hyperref[def:fol_signature]{first-order signature} with individual constants, for any \hyperref[def:fol_structure]{first-order structure} \( \mscrX = (X, I) \), either the universe \( X \) must be nonempty or the interpretation \( I \) must be a partial function.

  For \hyperref[def:higher_order_logic]{higher-order logic}, this is further amplified by the requirement that all universes in the frame \( X = \seq{ X_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} \) must be nonempty. An immediate peculiarity is that, if \( X_{\syn\iota} \) is empty, then the function universe \( X_{\tau \synimplies \syn\iota} \) is empty if and only if \( X_\tau \) is \hi{not} empty.

  We in first-order logic, we simply refrain from dealing with empty universes.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}\label{def:hol_variable_assignment}\mcite[238]{Andrews2002Logic}
  A \term{variable assignment} in a given \hyperref[def:hol_frame]{frame} \( X = \seq{ X_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} \) is a function \( v \) that sends each possible annotated variable \( x^\tau \) to an element of \( X_\tau \).

  For every variable \( x^\tau \) and every \( a \) from \( X_\tau \), we also define the \term{modified assignment} of \( v \) at \( x \) with \( a \):
  \begin{equation*}
    v_{x^\tau \mapsto a}(y^\tau) \coloneqq \begin{cases}
      a,         &y = x, \\
      v(y^\tau), &\T{otherwise.}
    \end{cases}
  \end{equation*}
\end{definition}
\begin{comments}
  \item We use Andrews' definition from \cite[238]{Andrews2002Logic}, but elaborate on modified assignments so that they more closely resemble their counterparts in first-order logic defined in \cref{def:fol_variable_assignment}.
\end{comments}

\begin{algorithm}[Higher-order logic denotation]\label{alg:hol_denotation}\mimprovised
  Fix a \hyperref[def:hol_structure]{structure} \( \mscrX = (X, I) \) of \hyperref[def:higher_order_logic]{higher-order logic}, as well as a \hyperref[def:hol_variable_assignment]{variable assignment} \( v \) into the frame \( X = \seq{ X_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} \).

  We define the \term{denotation} \( \Bracks{ M^\tau }_\mscrX^v \) of a generic \hyperref[def:hol_expression]{expression} \( M^\tau \) as follows:
  \begin{empheq}[left={\Bracks{ M^\tau }_\mscrX^v} \coloneqq \empheqlbrace]{align}
    &I(M),                                                                                          &&M \in \op*{NLConst},                                                     \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/nl_const} \\
    &v(M^\tau),                                                                                     &&M \in \op*{Var},                                                         \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/var} \\
    &\parens[\big]{ a \mapsto \Bracks{ N^\rho }_\mscrX^{v_{x^\sigma \mapsto a}} },                  &&\tau = \sigma \synimplies \rho \T{and} M^\tau = \qabs {x^\sigma} N^\rho, \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/abs} \\
    &\Bracks{ N^{\sigma \synimplies \tau} }_\mscrX^v \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{ K^\sigma }_\mscrX^v }, &&M^\tau = N^{\sigma \synimplies \tau} K^\sigma.                           \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/app}
  \end{empheq}

  Here \eqref{eq:alg:hol_denotation/abs} determines a function whose value for \( a \in X_\sigma \) is the denotation of \( N^\rho \) with respect to \( v \) modified at \( x^\sigma \) with \( a \). This ensures that the denotation of an arrow type is a function, so in \eqref{eq:alg:hol_denotation/app} we apply the denotation of the left (function) subterm to the denotation of the right subterm.

  Based on \fullref{alg:fol_formula_denotation}, we also define denotations specifically for the logical constants from \cref{def:hol_typing_rules}:
  \begin{empheq}[left={\Bracks{c}_\mscrX} \coloneqq \empheqlbrace]{align}
    &\semtop,                              &&c = \synH_\top,       \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/top} \\
    &\sembot,                              &&c = \synH_\bot,       \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/bot} \\
    &p \mapsto \oline{p},                  &&c = \synH_\neg,       \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/neg} \\
    &p \mapsto (q \mapsto (p \bincirc q)), &&c = \synH_{\syncirc}, \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/conn} \\
    &a \mapsto (b \mapsto \delta(a, b)),   &&c = \synH_=.          \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/eq}
  \end{empheq}

  Since we are working only with well-formed expressions, the denotations of standalone propositional constants like \( \synH_\top \) or \( \synH_\vee \) are always well-defined and do not depend on the structure \( \mscrX \) nor variable assignment \( v \).

  The denotation of \( \synH_= \) formally depends on the type of its arguments, but this is a negligible technicality since \( \synH_= \) always has two arguments in well-formed formulas.

  Quantifier formulas require special treatment. Well-formed quantifier formulas always have the form
  \begin{equation*}
    \quantifier Q {x^{\sigma}} \varphi = \synH_Q (\qabs {x^{\sigma}} \varphi).
  \end{equation*}

  The denotation of \( \synH_Q \) must be a function, but it is not clear how to define it. We can rely on its argument always being \( \Bracks{\qabs {x^\sigma} \varphi}_\mscrX^v \) for some abstraction \( \qabs {x^\sigma} \varphi \) and some assignment \( v \), which leads to the following definition:
  \begin{empheq}[left={\Bracks{c}_\mscrX^v} \coloneqq \empheqlbrace]{align}
    &\Bracks{\qabs {x^\sigma} \varphi}_\mscrX^v \mapsto \bigwedge\set[\big]{ \Bracks{\varphi}_\mscrX^{v^{x^\sigma \mapsto a}} \given* a \in X_\sigma }, &&c = \synH_\forall, \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/forall} \\
    &\Bracks{\qabs {x^\sigma} \varphi}_\mscrX^v \mapsto \bigvee\set[\big]{ \Bracks{\varphi}_\mscrX^{v^{x^\sigma \mapsto a}} \given* a \in X_\sigma }.   &&c = \synH_\exists. \label{eq:alg:hol_denotation/l_const/exists}
  \end{empheq}
\end{algorithm}
\begin{comments}
  \item We take the definition of denotation for arbitrary terms from \cite[238]{Andrews2002Logic}. For formulas, we generalize the denotations of first-order formulas from \fullref{alg:fol_formula_denotation}.

  \item As per \cref{rem:hol_parametrized_denotation}, due to the availability of \( \muplambda \)-abstraction, we will not be interested in parameterized denotations like in \cref{def:fol_parameterized_formula_denotation}.

  \item This algorithm can be found as \identifier{math.lambda_.hol.evaluate_hol_expression} in \cite{notebook:code}.
\end{comments}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hol_definite_description}
  Compared to Andrews and Farmer, we not only prefer the explicit typing rules from \cref{inf:def:hol_typing_rules} to the abbreviations from \cref{tab:rem:hol_formula_abbreviations}, but we also avoid the definite description operator \( \syndesc \) discussed in \cref{con:description_operator/iota}.

  Introducing this operator has some nontrivial consequences:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{rem:hol_definite_description/typing} Analogously to how we handled quantifiers in \cref{def:hol_typing_rules/quant}, we must introduce a new logical constant \( \synH_\turnediota \), as well as a typing rule
    \begin{equation*}\taglabel[\ensuremath{ \logic{H}_\turnediota }]{inf:rem:hol_definite_description/typing}
      \begin{prooftree}
        \hypo{ \synx: \syn\tau }
        \hypo{ \syn\varphi: \syn\omicron }
        \infer2[\ref{inf:rem:hol_definite_description/typing}]{ \underbrace{\synH_{\turnediota} (\qabs {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \syn\varphi)}_{\qdesc {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \syn\varphi}: \syn\tau }
      \end{prooftree}
    \end{equation*}

    This allows us to produce a term inhabiting any type.

    \thmitem{rem:hol_definite_description/interpretation} The interpretation of a \hyperref[def:hol_structure]{structure} must account for the possibility that no unique value assigned to \( \synx \) possibly satisfies \( \syn\varphi \).

    Farmer suggests endowing each structure \( \mscrX = (X, I) \) with a \hyperref[def:choice_function]{choice function} \( e \) on the frame \( X = \seq{ X_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_\Sigma}} \) that provides us with \enquote{error values}, i.e. fallback values for cases where unique existence fails\fnote{This is yet another reason to disallow empty universes.}.

    An alternative is to leave the denotation of \( \qdesc {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \syn\varphi \) \hyperref[con:undefinedness]{possibly undefined}. This is more complicated unfortunately because any value becomes possibly undefined. \incite*{Farmer1990PartialFunctionSTT} introduces 6 additional axioms to deal with such a situation.

    \thmitem{rem:hol_definite_description/axiom} \incite*[thm. 5404]{Andrews2002Logic} proves that, if we allow definite descriptions, every finite structure is standard.

    We will sketch his proof. Consider the arrow type \( \tau \synimplies \rho \). Fix any function \( g: X_\tau \to X_\rho \). We must show that \( g \) belongs to \( X_{\tau \synimplies \rho} \).

    Let \( a_1, \ldots, a_n \) be the elements of \( X_\tau \). Let \( v \) be any assignment such that \( v(\synb_k) = a_k \) and \( v(\sync_k) = g(a_k) \) for any \( k = 1, \ldots, n \).

    Andrews then proceeds to prove that \( g \) coincides with the denotation under \( v \) of the predicate
    \begin{equation*}
      \qabs {\synx^{\syn\tau}} \qdesc {\syny^{\syn\sigma}} (\synx \syneq \synb_1 \synwedge \syny \syneq \sync_1) \synvee \cdots \synvee (\synx \syneq \synb_n \synwedge \syny \syneq \sync_n).
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Translation of first-order logic}

\begin{algorithm}[First-order signature to higher-order signature]\label{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature}
  We will describe a procedure for converting a \hyperref[def:fol_signature]{first-order signature} \( \Sigma \) to a \hyperref[def:hol_signature]{higher-order signature} \( \Sigma' \).

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/sort} The only sort in \( \Sigma' \) is the type of individuals, \( \syn\iota \).
    \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/function} For every \( n \)-ary function symbol \( f \) in \( \Sigma \), we introduce into \( \Sigma' \) a nonlogical constant with the same name and with type
    \begin{equation*}
      \underbrace{\syn\iota \synimplies \cdots \synimplies \syn\iota}_{n \T*{times}} \synimplies \syn\iota.
    \end{equation*}

    \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/predicate} For every \( n \)-ary predicate symbol \( p \) in \( \Sigma \), we introduce into \( \Sigma' \) a nonlogical constant with the same name and with type
    \begin{equation*}
      \underbrace{\syn\iota \synimplies \cdots \synimplies \syn\iota}_{n \T*{times}} \synimplies \syn\omicron.
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{algorithm}
\begin{comments}
  \item This algorithm can be found as \identifier{math.lambda_.hol.fol_signature_to_hol_signature} in \cite{notebook:code}.
\end{comments}

\begin{algorithm}[First-order term to higher-order expression]\label{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression}
  Fix a \hyperref[def:fol_term]{first-order term} \( T \) over the \hyperref[def:fol_signature]{first-order signature} \( \Sigma \). Fix also a \hyperref[def:fol_structure]{first-order structure} \( \mscrX = (X, I) \) and a \hyperref[def:fol_variable_assignment]{variable assignment} \( v \) into \( \mscrX \).

  Via recursion on \( T \), we will translate \( T \) into a \hyperref[def:hol_expression]{well-formed expression} \( T' \):
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/variable} If \( T \) is a variable, say \( x \), simply let \( T' = x \).
    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/application} If \( T = f(S_1, \ldots, S_n) \), let \( S_k' \) be the translation of \( S_k \) and let
    \begin{equation*}
      T \coloneqq (\ldots ((f S_1) S_2) \ldots S_n).
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{algorithm}
\begin{comments}
  \item This translation is justified in \cref{thm:fol_to_hol_translation}.
  \item This algorithm can be found as \identifier{math.lambda_.hol.fol_term_to_hol_expression} in \cite{notebook:code}.
\end{comments}

\begin{algorithm}[First-order formula to higher-order expression]\label{alg:fol_formula_to_hol_expression}
  Fix a \hyperref[def:fol_formula]{first-order formula} \( \varphi \) over the \hyperref[def:fol_signature]{first-order signature} \( \Sigma \).

  Via recursion on \( \varphi \), we will translate \( \varphi \) into a \hyperref[def:hol_expression]{well-formed expression} \( \varphi' \):
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/verum} If \( \varphi = \syntop \), simply let \( \varphi' \coloneqq \synH_\top \).
    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/falsum} Similarly, if \( \varphi = \synbot \), simply let \( \varphi' \coloneqq \synH_\bot \).
    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/application} If \( \varphi = p(\psi_1, \ldots, \psi_n) \), let \( \psi'_k \) be the translation of \( \psi_k \) and let
    \begin{equation*}
      \varphi' \coloneqq (\ldots ((p \psi'_1) \psi'_2) \ldots \psi'_n).
    \end{equation*}

    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/connective} If \( \varphi = \psi \syncirc \theta \) , let \( \psi' \) and \( \theta' \) be the translations of \( \psi \) and \( \theta \), and let
    \begin{equation*}
      \varphi' \coloneqq (\synH_{\syncirc} \psi') \theta'.
    \end{equation*}

    \thmitem{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression/quantifier} If \( \varphi = \quantifier Q x \psi \) , let \( \psi' \) be the translations of \( \psi \), and let
    \begin{equation*}
      \varphi' \coloneqq \synH_Q (\qabs {x^{\syn\iota}} \psi').
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{algorithm}
\begin{comments}
  \item This translation is justified in \cref{thm:fol_to_hol_translation}.
  \item This algorithm can be found as \identifier{math.lambda_.hol.fol_formula_to_hol_expression} in \cite{notebook:code}.
\end{comments}

\begin{algorithm}[First-order structure to higher-order structure]\label{alg:fol_structure_to_hol_structure}
  Fix a \hyperref[def:fol_structure]{first-order structure} \( \mscrX = (X, I) \) over the \hyperref[def:fol_signature]{first-order signature} \( \Sigma \).

  we will translate \( \mscrX \) into a \hi{standard} \hyperref[def:hol_structure]{higher-order structure} \( \mscrX' = (X', I') \) as follows:
  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/frame} We define the frame \( X' = \seq{ X'_\tau }_{\tau \in \op*{\BbbQ_{\Sigma'}}} \) as follows:
    \begin{thmenum}
      \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/frame/individuals} For the type of individuals, we let \( X'_{\syn\iota} \) be the first-order universe \( X \).

      \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/frame/arrow} For the arrow type \( \tau \synimplies \sigma \), we define the universe \( X'_{\tau \synimplies \sigma} \) as the set of all functions from \( X'_\tau \) to \( X'_\sigma \).
    \end{thmenum}

    \thmitem{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/interpretation} We define interpretation \( I' \) as follows: for every \( n \)-ary function or predicate symbol \( s \) in \( \Sigma \), let \( I'(s) \) be \( I_s \), where
    \begin{equation*}
      I_{s,a_1,\ldots,a_k} \coloneqq \begin{cases}
        I(s)(a_1, \ldots, a_n),                       &k = n, \\
        a_{k+1} \mapsto I_{s,a_1,\ldots,a_k,a_{k+1}}, &k < n.
      \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{algorithm}
\begin{comments}
  \item It seems reasonable to define \( X'_{\tau \synimplies \sigma} \) as the set of only those functions from \( X'_\tau \) to \( X'_\sigma \) expressible in first-order logic. This would unfortunately leave \( X'_{\tau \synimplies \sigma} \) empty in many cases, for example if \( \tau = \syn\omicron \).

  \item This algorithm can be found as \identifier{math.lambda_.hol.fol_structure_to_hol_structure} in \cite{notebook:code}.
\end{comments}

\begin{proposition}\label{thm:fol_to_hol_translation}
  Fix a \hyperref[def:fol_structure]{first-order structure} \( \mscrX = (X, I) \) over the \hyperref[def:fol_signature]{first-order signature} \( \Sigma \). Fix also a \hyperref[def:fol_variable_assignment]{first-order variable assignment} \( v \) into \( \mscrX \).

  Let \( \Sigma' \) be the signature translated using \fullref{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature}, let \( \mscrX' \) be the structure translated using \fullref{alg:fol_structure_to_hol_structure} and let \( v' \) be any \hyperref[def:hol_variable_assignment]{higher-order assignment} that coincides with \( v \) on individuals.

  \begin{thmenum}
    \thmitem{thm:fol_to_hol_translation/term} For any \hyperref[def:fol_term]{first-order term} \( T \), for the translation \( T' \), obtained via \fullref{alg:fol_term_to_hol_expression}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
      \Bracks{T'}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} = \Bracks{T}_\mscrX^v.
    \end{equation*}

    \thmitem{thm:fol_to_hol_translation/formula} Similarly, for any \hyperref[def:fol_formula]{first-order formula} \( \varphi \), for the translation \( \varphi' \), obtained via \fullref{alg:fol_formula_to_hol_expression}, we have
    \begin{equation*}
      \Bracks{\varphi'}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} = \Bracks{\varphi}_\mscrX^v.
    \end{equation*}
  \end{thmenum}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  \SubProofOf{thm:fol_to_hol_translation/term} We proceed by \fullref{thm:induction_on_abstract_syntax} on \( T \) to show that \( \Bracks{T'}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} = \Bracks{T}_\mscrX^v \).
  \begin{itemize}
    \item If \( T \) is a variable, say \( x \), then \( v'(x^{\syn\iota}) = v(x) \) by assumption.
    \item If \( T = f(S_1, \ldots, S_n) \), where the inductive hypothesis holds for \( S_1, \ldots, S_n \), then
    \begin{balign*}
      \Bracks{T'}_{\mscrX'}^{v'}
      &=
      \Bracks{(\ldots ((f S'_1) S'_2) \ldots S'_n)}_{\mscrX'}^{v'}
      \reloset {\eqref{eq:alg:hol_denotation/app}} = \\ &=
      \Bracks{f}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S'_1}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} } \ldots \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S'_n}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} }
      \reloset {\T{ind.}} = \\ &=
      \Bracks{f}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S_1}_\mscrX^v } \ldots \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S_n}_\mscrX^v }
      \reloset {\eqref{eq:alg:hol_denotation/abs}} = \\ &=
      I'(f) \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S_1}_\mscrX^v } \ldots \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S_n}_\mscrX^v }
      \reloset {\ref{alg:fol_signature_to_hol_signature/interpretation}} = \\ &=
      I(f) \parens[\big]{ \Bracks{S_1}_\mscrX^v, \ldots, \Bracks{S_n}_\mscrX^v }
      = \\ &=
      \Bracks{T}_\mscrX^v.
    \end{balign*}
  \end{itemize}

  \SubProofOf{thm:fol_to_hol_translation/formula} We can use induction on the formula \( \varphi \), similarly to \cref{thm:fol_to_hol_translation/term}, to show that \( \Bracks{\varphi'}_{\mscrX'}^{v'} = \Bracks{\varphi}_\mscrX^v \).
\end{proof}
